Lesson 0001 · Orientation

The RL Landscape: Where Everything Fits

A map of the field, so every algorithm you meet from here on has a slot to go in.

Why this lesson

Your job spans general RL with a lean toward controller optimization and simulated agents, and you want paper fluency on top. Before any single algorithm is worth learning deeply, you need the coordinate system that tells you where it sits relative to everything else — that's what lets you place a new algorithm or paper in seconds instead of re-deriving your understanding from scratch each time.

The core loop

Almost everything in RL is one loop, repeated: an agent observes a state, takes an action, and the environment returns a reward and a new state. The agent's entire objective is to choose actions to maximize the reward it accumulates over time — not just the next reward, but the expected sum of all future rewards (usually discounted, so sooner reward counts more).

Formally: a policy π(a|s) maps states to actions (or a distribution over them); the goal is to find the π that maximizes expected discounted return E[Σ γᵗrₜ]. This is the entire objective of the field, stated precisely. We'll build up the full formalism (MDPs, value functions) in the next lesson — for now, hold onto the shape of the objective, not the notation.

Two axes that organize almost everything

Every RL algorithm you'll meet can be placed on two largely independent axes. Knowing them turns "what is this new paper doing" into a quick lookup instead of a mystery.

Axis 1 — Model-free vs. model-based

  • Model-free: the agent never learns an explicit model of how the environment works. It learns purely from the reward signal produced by experience — trial, error, update.
  • Model-based: the agent learns (or is given) a model that predicts "if I do this, what happens next" — and uses it to plan or generate imagined experience, which is often far more sample-efficient.

Axis 2 — How the policy is represented

  • Value-based: learn how good each action is (a Q-function); the policy falls out by picking the best-valued action. No explicit policy is stored.
  • Policy-based: directly parametrize and optimize the policy itself (e.g. with a neural network and gradient ascent on expected return).
  • Actor-critic: learn both at once — a policy ("actor") and a value function ("critic") that reduces the noise in the actor's gradient estimates. Most modern deep RL used in practice lives here.

Your corner of the map

Controller optimization and simulated-agent work overwhelmingly lives in actor-critic, model-free methods (PPO, SAC) run at massive parallelism on GPU-native simulators — Brax, MJX, gymnax, PureJaxRL. Model-based RL shows up when simulation itself is the bottleneck. One adjacent, non-RL family worth knowing by name: direct policy-search/evolutionary methods (e.g. CMA-ES) — sometimes used for controller optimization instead of RL entirely, especially when the reward landscape is smooth and dimensionality is low.
