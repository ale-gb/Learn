Scene vs. Summary: A Working Checklist

Print this. Keep it next to the draft. Use it every time a passage feels either rushed or bloated.

The core question

Every stretch of narrative is doing one of two jobs. Naming which job it's doing is the first move in any pacing fix.

SceneSummary
TimeReal time, roughly 1:1Compressed — hours, weeks, years in a paragraph
ModeDramatized: action, dialogue, sensory detailNarrated: told, reported, summarized
Reader feels itYes — happening to them, liveNo — informed about it, at a distance
Use forThe moments that change something (a decision, a relationship, a reveal)The connective tissue between those moments

Three questions to ask a passage

  1. Does this moment change the character's situation, decision, or relationship in a way the reader needs to feel happening? If yes, it probably wants to be a scene, not a summary.
  2. Is this mainly information the reader needs but doesn't need to live through? If yes, summary is doing its job — don't dramatize it just because "showing" sounds more virtuous than "telling." Summary is a craft choice, not a failure.
  3. Am I already dramatizing something that doesn't earn the real-time treatment? A scene that isn't doing structural work (see below) is usually the fix in the other direction — compress it.

The compress-then-zoom move

The most common working pattern in published fiction — and the one this workspace's Lesson 0001 is built around — is not "all scene" or "all summary." It's summarizing the buildup and then dropping into a fully dramatized scene at the exact moment things turn:

Buildup (summary) → establish the situation and its drift, compressed to a paragraph or two → Turn (scene) → the instant the situation actually changes, given full sensory, real-time treatment.

Scene shape, once you're in one (Swain's Scene/Sequel)

Scene (action)Sequel (reaction)
Goal — character wants something entering the sceneReaction — emotional fallout from what just happened
Conflict — something opposes the goalDilemma — no good option is obvious
Disaster / turn — the goal fails, or succeeds at a costDecision — a new goal forms, feeding the next scene

Not every scene needs the sequel spelled out on the page — but if a scene feels like it "just ends," check whether the character ever got a beat to react before the story moves on.
