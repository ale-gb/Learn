Lesson 0001 · Pacing & Scene Craft

Scene vs. Summary: Choosing Where to Slow Down

The single decision that shapes pacing more than any other — and one you're already making well in at least one place in your own work.

Why this lesson

You named pacing/scene craft and character/dialogue as where you most want to grow, and you're mid-draft on a novella that interleaves several timelines. Before touching that structure, the more basic question underneath it is: for any given stretch of story, did you decide to dramatize it or compress it — and was that the right call? Get this right and half of what reads as "pacing problems" disappears on its own.

Two modes, one decision

Almost every stretch of prose is doing one of two jobs:

  • Scene — dramatized, roughly real-time, sensory. The reader lives through it as it happens: action, dialogue, physical detail.
  • Summary — narrated, compressed. The reader is told what happened, often across hours, weeks, or years, in a paragraph or two.

Neither is "better." Summary isn't a failure to show — it's the connective tissue that makes scenes mean something by giving them context and momentum. The craft is choosing correctly which moments earn the real-time treatment.

You already do this — watch yourself do it

In Un'ultima cosa bella, the narrator's friend tells the story of how his marriage nearly ended. The buildup — an intern he's mentoring develops feelings for him — is handled entirely in summary:

«Toccava a lui occuparsene e farle da mentore. All'inizio era entusiasta della cosa... Più passavano i giorni, però, più si accorgeva che c'era qualcosa di profondamente sbagliato... E la cosa peggiore era che lei non solo se n'era accorta, ma ricambiava. E non solo ricambiava, ma sembrava determinata a ottenere ciò che voleva.» Un'ultima cosa bella — mentorship buildup, narrated in summary across weeks

Gloss: weeks of a mentorship curdling into mutual attraction, entirely told, not shown — correctly. Nothing here needs to be lived through in real time; it's setup.

Then the story reaches the office Christmas party — the night it actually happens — and without announcing the shift, the prose drops straight into scene: real time, sensory detail, dialogue.

«Nel suo vestito dorato, scintillava come una delle decorazioni che riempivano la sala... Mi fissò negli occhi per un momento, poi avvicinò le labbra all'orecchio, mentre con la mano libera afferrava il frutto del suo incantesimo, poco sotto la cintura: "Lo voglio. Adesso." sussurrò, con una voce rubata a una sirena.» Un'ultima cosa bella — the party, dramatized in full scene

Gloss: same event class as the passage above, but this is the turn — the moment the situation actually changes — so it gets sight, sound, touch, and dialogue.

That's the whole technique in miniature: summarize the buildup, dramatize the turn. You already reached for it here without being taught it. The goal of this lesson isn't to introduce a new move — it's to name the one you have, so you can reach for it on purpose in places where you're currently staying in summary a beat too long, or dropping into scene where summary would serve better.

Scene, once you're in one, has a shape

Dwight Swain's "Scene/Sequel" model (see primary source below) gives scenes a three-beat shape: Goal → Conflict → Disaster (the turn). Your party scene has all three: the narrator's friend has an implicit goal to survive the night without incident, the intern's approach is the conflict, and giving in is the disaster — the goal fails, or rather, succeeds at a cost he immediately regrets ("Me ne pentii nel momento stesso in cui venni dentro di lei"). That regret, right after, is the start of the sequel — the character's reaction, which is its own beat and earns its own space on the page.

Your turn: find the moment that's still in summary

Right before the party scene, the story tells us — but never shows us — the exact instant the narrator's friend first realizes the intern reciprocates:

«Più passavano i giorni, però, più si accorgeva che c'era qualcosa di profondamente sbagliato; che le sensazioni che sempre più spesso provava verso di lei erano tutt'altro che quelle di un padre verso la figlia. E la cosa peggiore era che lei non solo se n'era accorta, ma ricambiava.»

Exercise (bring it next session): Pick either that moment, or a comparably summarized beat from your own novella draft, and dramatize it as a short scene — 150 to 300 words, real time, at least one line of dialogue. Don't worry about whether it would actually go in the final draft. The point is practicing the zoom, not producing final prose.

Primary source for next time

Read K.M. Weiland's "How to Structure Scenes in Your Story" series — a free, thorough walkthrough of the Scene/Sequel model with worked literary examples, going deeper than this lesson does. If you want the older, denser primary source, that's Dwight V. Swain's Techniques of the Selling Writer (1965), where the technique originates.

Not sure whether a specific passage in your draft should be scene or summary? Bring the exact passage next session and ask — that's a much more useful question than it might feel like, and it's exactly what this workspace is for.
