Lesson 0002 · Connections & Bundles

Two Faces of a Connection: Parallel Transport = Covariant Derivative

The covariant derivative can be defined two ways — as an axiomatic operator (Schuller's Leibniz rule) or as a limit of parallel transport. They are the same object. Here is the proof — without the circular shortcut that smuggles in Christoffel symbols.

Why this lesson

Lesson 0001 set up a connection as the rule for comparing fibres at neighbouring points. There are two standard ways to package that rule: the integral one (parallel-transport a vector back and subtract) and the differential one (Schuller's axioms). It is tempting to "prove" they agree by writing both in a chart and matching the \(\Gamma\)'s — but that is circular. This lesson gives the honest, coordinate-free equivalence, and shows that \(\Gamma\) is a shadow that never enters the real argument.

The two definitions

Differential (Schuller's axioms). A connection is a map \(\nabla\) sending a vector field \(X\) and a vector field \(Y\) to a vector field \(\nabla_X Y\), satisfying

  • \(C^\infty\)-linearity in \(X\): \(\nabla_{fX}Y = f\,\nabla_X Y\) (and additivity in \(X\));
  • additivity in \(Y\): \(\nabla_X(Y+Z) = \nabla_X Y + \nabla_X Z\);
  • the Leibniz rule: \(\nabla_X(fY) = X(f)\,Y + f\,\nabla_X Y\).

Integral (parallel transport). Suppose instead we are handed, for every curve \(\gamma\), a parallel-transport map \(\Pi_{\gamma,h}: T_{P}M \to T_{\gamma(h)}M\) (with \(P=\gamma(0)\)) that is (i) a linear isomorphism, (ii) the identity at \(h=0\), (iii) smooth in \(h\). Its inverse carries a vector at \(\gamma(h)\) back to \(P\), where it can be compared with \(Y_P\). Define

\[ D_X Y\big|_P \;=\; \lim_{h\to 0}\frac{\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}\big(Y_{\gamma(h)}\big) - Y_P}{h}, \qquad X = \dot\gamma(0). \]

The claim is that these define the same structure. To prove it honestly we treat them as two a priori independent objects and show each implies the other.

Why the quick "proof" is circular: write the axiomatic \(\nabla\) in a chart to get \(\nabla_X Y = \big(X(Y^k) + \Gamma^{k}{}_{ij}X^iY^j\big)\partial_k\); define transport as the solution of \(\dot V^k + \Gamma^{k}{}_{ij}\dot\gamma^i V^j = 0\); plug it into the limit and — unsurprisingly — recover the same \(\Gamma\). That is \(\nabla\to\Gamma\to\Pi\to\Gamma\to\nabla\): you assumed the link you set out to prove. So we forbid \(\Gamma\) entirely.

Part 1 — transport implies the axioms

Take the integral derivative \(D\) and show it obeys Schuller's axioms. The Leibniz rule is the revealing one, so watch it fall out with nothing but linearity of \(\Pi^{-1}\).

\[ D_X(fY)\big|_P = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}\big(f(\gamma(h))\,Y_{\gamma(h)}\big) - f(P)\,Y_P}{h}. \]

Because \(\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}\) is linear (Property i), the scalar \(f(\gamma(h))\) pulls straight through it:

\[ \Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}\big(f(\gamma(h))\,Y_{\gamma(h)}\big) = f(\gamma(h))\,\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}\big(Y_{\gamma(h)}\big). \]

Now add and subtract \(f(P)\,\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}Y_{\gamma(h)}\) in the numerator and split:

\[ D_X(fY)\big|_P = \lim_{h\to0}\underbrace{\frac{f(\gamma(h))-f(P)}{h}}_{\to\,X(f)\,|_P}\;\underbrace{\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}Y_{\gamma(h)}}_{\to\,Y_P} \;+\; f(P)\,\lim_{h\to0}\frac{\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}Y_{\gamma(h)} - Y_P}{h}. \]

The first bracket is the directional derivative of the function \(f\) along \(\gamma\), namely \(X(f)\), and the transported vector tends to \(Y_P\) (transport \(\to\) identity as \(h\to0\)). The second limit is exactly \(D_X Y|_P\). Hence

\[ \boxed{\,D_X(fY)\big|_P = X(f)\,Y_P + f(P)\,D_X Y\big|_P\,}. \]

That is the Leibniz axiom — and \(\Gamma\) never appeared. Additivity in \(Y\) follows the same way from linearity of \(\Pi^{-1}\); and one checks the limit depends only on the tangent vector \(X_P=\dot\gamma(0)\) (not on the rest of the curve) and is linear in it, giving the \(C^\infty\)-linearity in \(X\). So every parallel transport defines an axiomatic connection.

Part 2 — the axioms imply transport

Now start from an axiomatic \(\nabla\) with no transport in sight, and manufacture the transport map from it — then check the limit formula reproduces \(\nabla\).

Build transport from an ODE. Call a vector field \(V(t)\) along \(\gamma\) parallel if

\[ \nabla_{\dot\gamma}\,V = 0. \]

This is a linear, first-order ODE along the curve; by the standard existence–uniqueness theorem it has a unique solution for each initial vector \(v_0\in T_PM\). Define \(\tilde\Pi_{\gamma}^{\,0,t}(v_0) := V(t)\). Linearity of the ODE makes \(\tilde\Pi\) a vector-space isomorphism — a parallel transport, produced purely from \(\nabla\).

Feed it back into the limit. Pick a parallel frame \(\{E_i(t)\}\) along \(\gamma\) (each \(\nabla_{\dot\gamma}E_i = 0\); they exist by transporting a basis of \(T_PM\)), and expand \(Y(t) = y^i(t)\,E_i(t)\). By additivity and Leibniz,

\[ \nabla_{\dot\gamma}Y = \dot y^i(t)\,E_i(t) + y^i(t)\underbrace{\nabla_{\dot\gamma}E_i}_{=\,0} = \dot y^i(t)\,E_i(t). \]

Since the frame is parallel, \(E_i(h) = \tilde\Pi_{\gamma,h}E_i(0)\), so transporting \(Y_{\gamma(h)}\) back to \(P\) merely holds its components fixed in that frame:

\[ \tilde\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}\,Y_{\gamma(h)} = \tilde\Pi_{\gamma,h}^{-1}\big(y^i(h)\,E_i(h)\big) = y^i(h)\,E_i(0). \]

Drop this into the integral definition:

\[ D_X Y\big|_P = \lim_{h\to0}\frac{y^i(h)\,E_i(0) - y^i(0)\,E_i(0)}{h} = \dot y^i(0)\,E_i(0) = \big(\nabla_{\dot\gamma}Y\big)\big|_P. \]

The limit built from the ODE-generated transport lands exactly on the axiomatic derivative. So every axiomatic connection defines a parallel transport whose transport-limit is that same connection.

The one idea

Parallel transport (integral) and Schuller's axioms (differential) are two presentations of the same structure — an affine connection. Transport \(\Rightarrow\) Leibniz by linearity of \(\Pi^{-1}\); the axioms \(\Rightarrow\) transport by solving \(\nabla_{\dot\gamma}V=0\). Neither direction uses a chart, so the equivalence is genuine, not circular. The Christoffel symbols \(\Gamma\) are only the coordinate shadow that appears when you force either formulation into a chart — secondary to the geometry, never part of the proof.

What this buys you

The "integral vs differential" pairing is the same relationship as antiderivative vs derivative, or holonomy vs curvature: one accumulates the rule along a whole curve, the other reads it off infinitesimally. Having both is practical — Leibniz-style manipulations are easy in the differential picture, while global statements (holonomy, the effect of going around a loop) are natural in the transport picture. And it retires the worry that a connection is "really" its Christoffel symbols: \(\Gamma\) is a chart-dependent bookkeeping device for one and the same coordinate-free object.
