Lesson 0001 · Connections & Bundles

Covariant Derivatives Live on Bundles

Why \(\nabla_X f = X(f)\) for an ordinary function, yet the wavefunction needs a genuine correction term — and why the deciding factor is not that \(\psi\) is complex, but that it is charged.

The puzzle

In Schuller's lecture on connections he builds a covariant derivative \(\nabla\) and requires that on a function it collapses to the plain directional derivative, \(\nabla_X f = X(f)\). Elsewhere he says the QM wavefunction is a complex field on spacetime that does need a covariant derivative to compare values at neighbouring points. Contradiction? If \(\nabla\) is trivial on functions, why is \(\psi\) — which also looks like a function \(M\to\mathbb{C}\) — any different? Is it because it's complex? This lesson resolves it, and the resolution is one of the most useful ideas in geometric physics.

One manifold, many bundles — and a connection lives on just one

The hidden assumption in the puzzle is that "\(\nabla\)" is a single universal operator. It isn't. A covariant derivative is always a derivative of sections of one specified vector bundle, and it comes with a connection tailored to that bundle.

A vector bundle \(E \xrightarrow{\pi} M\) attaches to each point \(x\in M\) a fibre \(E_x\) — a vector space — and a section is a field assigning to each \(x\) a vector in \(E_x\). Different bundles over the same spacetime:

  • the tangent bundle \(TM\): fibres are tangent spaces, sections are vector fields;
  • the trivial line bundle \(M\times\mathbb{R}\): fibres are copies of \(\mathbb{R}\), sections are ordinary functions \(f:M\to\mathbb{R}\);
  • a complex line bundle \(E\): fibres are 1-dimensional complex spaces \(\cong\mathbb{C}\), sections are wavefunctions.

Here is the crux. The fibres over two different points are different vector spaces. To differentiate a section you must compare \(s(x)\) and \(s(x+dx)\) — but they live in \(E_x\) and \(E_{x+dx}\), and there is no God-given way to subtract vectors from different spaces. A connection is precisely the extra structure that supplies the missing rule: how to parallel-transport a fibre element from \(x+dx\) back to \(x\) so the comparison makes sense. And a connection is defined on a particular bundle — the connection on \(TM\) (the Christoffel symbols \(\Gamma\)) knows how to carry tangent vectors and says nothing whatever about any other bundle.

Why a function needs no correction term

Now Schuller's axiom \(\nabla_X f = X(f)\). A scalar function is a section of the trivial line bundle \(M\times\mathbb{R}\). "Trivial" is the load-bearing word: its fibres come with a canonical identification — every fibre is literally the same \(\mathbb{R}\), with the same distinguished number \(1\). Because neighbouring fibres are already identified, comparing \(f(x+dx)\) with \(f(x)\) is just subtraction; no transport rule is needed and no correction term appears:

\[ \nabla_X f = X(f) = \mathrm{d}f(X). \]

Put physically: a bare number carries no internal "direction" that could get rotated as you move from point to point, so there is nothing for a connection to correct. In Schuller's construction this is the base case (functions are \((0,0)\)-tensors); the Leibniz rule then propagates \(\nabla\) up to all tensor fields, where the \(\Gamma\)'s do appear because tangent vectors do point in internal directions. So \(\nabla_X f = X(f)\) is not a universal fact about functions — it is the statement that the scalar bundle is trivially connected.

The wavefunction lives on a different bundle

Written \(\psi(x)\in\mathbb{C}\), the wavefunction impersonates a function — but geometrically it is a section of a complex line bundle \(E\) carrying a local \(\mathrm{U}(1)\) structure. The physics that makes the bundle non-trivial is local phase (gauge) symmetry: you may rotate the phase independently at every point,

\[ \psi(x)\;\longrightarrow\;e^{i\alpha(x)}\,\psi(x),\qquad \alpha(x)\ \text{arbitrary at each } x. \]

Because that choice is free at every point, there is no canonical sense in which \(\psi(x)\) and \(\psi(x+dx)\) have "the same phase" — unlike the trivial scalar bundle, these fibres are not pre-identified. So to differentiate you must supply a transport rule for phase: a connection on this bundle. That connection is the gauge potential \(A_\mu\) — physically, the electromagnetic vector potential — and the covariant derivative is written \(D\) to keep it distinct from the spacetime \(\nabla\):

\[ D_\mu\psi = \partial_\mu\psi + iqA_\mu\,\psi. \]

The extra piece \(iqA_\mu\psi\) is exactly the parallel-transport correction for phase. It earns the name "covariant" because \(A_\mu\) transforms in concert with \(\psi\): a field of charge \(q\) responds to the local phase change as \(\psi\to e^{iq\alpha(x)}\psi\), the potential shifts as \(A_\mu\to A_\mu-\partial_\mu\alpha\), and then

\[ D_\mu\psi \;\longrightarrow\; e^{iq\alpha(x)}\,D_\mu\psi, \]

so \(D_\mu\psi\) rotates exactly the way \(\psi\) does — which is what lets you build gauge-invariant physics from it. (The plain \(\partial_\mu\psi\) does not transform this way; that mismatch is the whole reason \(D\) is needed — and, as we'll pin down below, it is also what "charge" really means.)

So — is it because it's complex? No: it's because it's charged

This is the heart of your question, and the way to settle it is two clean test cases.

Test 1 — a complex field that needs no connection

Take a complex scalar with charge \(q=0\) (an uncharged field). It is fully complex, yet its \(\mathrm{U}(1)\) phase is only a global symmetry, not a gauged one. The bundle is canonically trivial, the connection is flat (\(A_\mu=0\)), and

\[ D_\mu\phi = \partial_\mu\phi. \]

Just like a real function. So being complex is not what forces a correction term — a free, uncharged wavefunction uses the plain \(\partial_\mu\) too.

Test 2 — a real field that does need one

Take a doublet of real scalars \(\vec\phi=(\phi_1,\phi_2)^{\mathsf T}\) with a local \(\mathrm{SO}(2)\) gauge symmetry — you may rotate the pair by an arbitrary angle at each point. The fields are strictly real, but they are sections of a non-trivial rank-2 real bundle, so you must introduce an \(\mathrm{SO}(2)\) connection \(A_\mu\) (a Lie-algebra-valued, i.e. antisymmetric, matrix):

\[ D_\mu\vec\phi = \partial_\mu\vec\phi + A_\mu\,\vec\phi. \]

Real fields, genuine connection. The pattern is unmistakable:

The one idea

There is no universal covariant derivative — a connection is extra structure attached to a specific bundle. \(\nabla_X f = X(f)\) says the scalar bundle is trivially connected; the wavefunction's bundle is not, so it needs its own connection, the gauge field. What forces a non-trivial correction term is that the field is charged under a local gauge symmetry (a non-trivial bundle) — not whether its components are real or complex.

The missing link: what "charged" actually means

Fair objection at this point: I keep saying "charged" and "transforms under the local gauge symmetry" as if they're the same thing, without saying why. Here's the link, and it's almost a definition once you see where the charge sits.

The charge is how much the field rotates

There is one gauge freedom — a single function \(\alpha(x)\). But different fields respond to it by different amounts. A field of charge \(q\) transforms as

\[ \psi(x)\;\longrightarrow\;e^{\,iq\,\alpha(x)}\,\psi(x). \]

The charge \(q\) is nothing more than how much that field's phase turns under the symmetry — its "weight." An electron carries \(q=-e\), an up quark \(q=+\tfrac23 e\), a neutrino \(q=0\). So "charged" and "transforms under the local phase symmetry" aren't two separate facts to be connected: they are the same fact, and \(q\) is the number that measures it.

Uncharged means it doesn't transform

Set \(q=0\): then \(\psi\to e^{\,i\cdot 0\cdot\alpha}\psi=\psi\), unchanged. The field is inert under the symmetry — it sits in the trivial representation. A single real field is stuck at \(q=0\), because \(e^{iq\alpha}\) would turn a real number complex; that's why a lone real scalar is always neutral. A real doublet, though, can be genuinely rotated by \(\mathrm{SO}(2)\) at each point — that rotation is a real transformation, so it carries charge despite being real. (A charge-1 complex field and an \(\mathrm{SO}(2)\) real doublet are the very same object in different clothes.)

Why transforming forces the connection — with the same \(q\)

Now differentiate, and watch where the charge reappears. Because \(\alpha\) depends on position,

\[ \partial_\mu\big(e^{\,iq\alpha}\psi\big) = e^{\,iq\alpha}\big(\partial_\mu\psi + iq\,(\partial_\mu\alpha)\,\psi\big). \]

That stray \(iq\,(\partial_\mu\alpha)\,\psi\) is exactly what breaks covariance — and only a field with \(q\neq 0\) produces it. The compensating gauge term that cancels it is \(iqA_\mu\psi\), carrying the same \(q\). So the number measuring "how much you rotate under the symmetry" is identical to the number measuring "how strongly you couple to \(A_\mu\)." One quantity, two hats — that identity is the whole answer to your question.

And that same \(q\) is the electric charge

Step back for the punchline. A complex field with ordinary phase-invariant dynamics already has a global \(\mathrm{U}(1)\) symmetry (rotate by a constant \(\alpha\)), and by Noether's theorem that hands you a conserved charge. "Gauging" the symmetry means demanding the phase convention be a purely local choice — and that demand is what forces the new field \(A_\mu\) into existence. Once it does, the previously merely-conserved charge becomes the coupling to \(A_\mu\): the electric charge that sources electromagnetism and feels the Lorentz force. The one number \(q\) is therefore simultaneously the weight under the symmetry, the Noether charge, and the physical electric charge.

So "charged, not complex" is no longer mysterious: complexness merely supplies a phase that could be rotated — a place for a charge to live. Charge is whether it actually rotates under the local symmetry (\(q\neq 0\)), and that is precisely the condition that makes \(\partial_\mu\) fail and a connection necessary. A neutral field — real, or complex with \(q=0\) — sits inert in the trivial representation and needs none.

For a non-abelian gauge group the same story generalizes: "charge" becomes which representation the field sits in (the trivial rep = uncharged; the fundamental, adjoint, … = various charges), and the transformation \(\psi\to e^{iq\alpha}\psi\) becomes \(\psi\to U(x)\,\psi\) with \(U(x)\) a local group element in that representation. The covariant derivative then carries a matrix-valued connection \(A_\mu\). Same principle, richer bookkeeping — this is the road to Yang–Mills and the Standard Model.

But if it's a symmetry, how do you "measure" the rotation?

Good worry — and the loose phrase "how much it rotates" is what invites it. You cannot measure the rotation itself: the absolute phase of \(\psi\) is unobservable (only \(|\psi|^2\) and relative phases are physical), and a local gauge transformation is a pure redundancy of description — different gauge choices label the very same physical state, so there is simply nothing there to measure. That instinct is right.

The resolution is that \(q\) is a label on the transformation law, not a measured event — exactly like spin. Rotational symmetry leaves physics invariant, yet "spin \(j\)" still labels how a field transforms under rotations, and spin is thoroughly measurable — not by watching a scalar "rotate" (it doesn't), but through its consequences. Charge is to phase symmetry what spin is to rotational symmetry: a representation weight, read off from consequences, never from watching the symmetry act.

And those consequences are fixed by the very same \(q\) — the one in \(D_\mu=\partial_\mu+iqA_\mu\): the conserved Noether charge of the global \(\mathrm{U}(1)\) (what you measure in coulombs); the Lorentz force \(\propto q\); and, most sharply, the Aharonov–Bohm effect. Send a charge-\(q\) particle in superposition around a region enclosing magnetic flux \(\Phi\); the two paths pick up a relative phase

\[ \Delta\varphi = q\,\Phi, \]

which — unlike an absolute phase — is gauge-invariant and physical, and shifts the interference fringes. Vary \(\Phi\) and the fringes march at a rate \(q\): you are, in effect, measuring "how much the phase turned," but the gauge-invariant relative version that survives the symmetry. So the symmetry being a symmetry is exactly why the rotation is invisible, while \(q\) lives on as measurable through the footprints it dictates — deflections, fringe shifts, conserved charge. "Charge = how much it rotates" is shorthand for "\(q\) is the field's \(\mathrm{U}(1)\) transformation weight," made real by the coupling it forces.

The unifying picture

Christoffel symbols and the electromagnetic potential are the same kind of object — connections — living on different bundles, and their curvatures are cousins too.

Tangent bundle \(TM\)Gauge \(\mathrm{U}(1)\) line bundle \(E\)
Sectionsvector / tensor fieldsthe wavefunction \(\psi\)
Local symmetrychange of frame (diffeomorphisms)local phase \(\psi\to e^{i\alpha(x)}\psi\)
ConnectionChristoffel \(\Gamma^{\lambda}{}_{\mu\nu}\) (Levi-Civita)potential \(A_\mu\) (electromagnetism)
Covariant derivative\(\nabla_\mu V^\nu = \partial_\mu V^\nu + \Gamma^{\nu}{}_{\mu\lambda}V^\lambda\)\(D_\mu\psi = \partial_\mu\psi + iqA_\mu\psi\)
On a scalar\(\nabla_X f = X(f)\) (trivial)\(D_\mu\phi = \partial_\mu\phi\) if \(q=0\) (trivial)
CurvatureRiemann \(R^{\rho}{}_{\sigma\mu\nu}\)field strength \(F_{\mu\nu}=\partial_\mu A_\nu-\partial_\nu A_\mu\)

Both curvatures measure the same thing — the failure of a vector (or a phase) to return to itself after parallel transport around a small closed loop. The electromagnetic field is the curvature of the \(\mathrm{U}(1)\) connection, exactly as the Riemann tensor is the curvature of the spacetime connection.

And the punchline that ties the whole taxonomy together: if a field carries both spacetime indices and gauge charge — a charged vector boson, say — its covariant derivative must include both connections at once:

\[ D_\mu V^\nu = \partial_\mu V^\nu + \underbrace{\Gamma^{\nu}{}_{\mu\lambda}V^\lambda}_{\text{spacetime}} + \underbrace{iqA_\mu V^\nu}_{\text{gauge}}. \]

You hit each index with the connection belonging to the bundle that index lives on. That single rule dissolves the original confusion completely.
