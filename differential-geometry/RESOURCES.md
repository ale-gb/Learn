# Differential Geometry for Physics — Resources

High-trust sources for the geometry underlying modern physics, curated for a concept-first
learner with real mathematical maturity who wants rigor *and* physical grounding.

## Knowledge — courses (rigorous, physics-facing)

- [Lectures: **Geometrical Anatomy of Theoretical Physics** — Frederic Schuller](https://www.youtube.com/playlist?list=PLPH7f_7ZlzxTi6kS4vCmv4ZKm9u8g5yic)
  The workspace's anchor course, and the source of the question behind lesson 0001. Utterly
  precise, self-contained development from logic and set theory through manifolds, bundles,
  connections, and gauge theory. Schuller's insistence on stating exactly *which* structure an
  object lives on is precisely the habit this workspace is built around.
  - [Lecture notes (transcribed by Simon Rea)](https://tales.mbivert.com/Lectures_on_Geometric_Anatomy_of_Theoretical_Physics.pdf) — a clean, corrected written version of the whole course; the fastest way to look up a definition Schuller gave.
  - The specific video behind lesson 0001 (connections): [youtu.be/nEaiZBbCVtI](https://youtu.be/nEaiZBbCVtI).

## Knowledge — reference texts

- [Book: **Geometry, Topology and Physics** — M. Nakahara](https://www.routledge.com/Geometry-Topology-and-Physics/Nakahara/p/book/9780750306065)
  The standard physicist's reference. Use for: bundles, connections, characteristic classes,
  and the index-friendly bridge between the abstract geometry and gauge-theory/GR calculations.
- [Book: **Gauge Fields, Knots and Gravity** — J. Baez &amp; J. Muniain](https://www.worldscientific.com/worldscibook/10.1142/2324)
  The friendliest rigorous route into connections and curvature *as gauge theory*, built to
  make "electromagnetism is a U(1) connection" feel inevitable. Ideal companion for the
  bundle/connection lessons.
- [Book: **The Geometry of Physics: An Introduction** — T. Frankel](https://www.cambridge.org/highereducation/books/the-geometry-of-physics/E0491199D6C0C0A87FCD0961172D089B)
  Broad, example-rich coverage of forms, Lie groups, bundles, and their physics uses. Good for
  a second, more geometric-intuition-driven angle on any topic.
- [Notes: **Differential Geometry** and **General Relativity** — David Tong](http://www.damtp.cam.ac.uk/user/tong/diffgeom.html)
  Concise, physicist-friendly notes on manifolds, tensors, connections, and curvature — a
  quicker read than the full texts when you just need the working picture.
