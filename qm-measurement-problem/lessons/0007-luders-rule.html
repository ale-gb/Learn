Lüders' Rule: The State After a Measurement

The Born rule tells you the probabilities of outcomes. Lüders' rule tells you the state that's left behind — and, for degenerate observables, it is the uniquely gentle answer: erase only what the measurement could distinguish, disturb nothing else.

Why this lesson

Every earlier lesson leaned on "the state collapses onto the measured outcome." For a sharp (non-degenerate) observable that's unambiguous. But when eigenvalues are degenerate, the naive rule is ill-defined, and von Neumann's 1932 prescription gives a basis-dependent answer. Gerhart Lüders (1951) found the correct, unique rule. It is worth doing precisely — it's what "collapse" actually is, it's derivable from unitary quantum mechanics plus the Born rule, and it has been tested.

The setup and the gap Lüders filled

Write an observable through its spectral decomposition into eigenprojectors,

\[ A = \sum_k a_k\,P_k, \qquad P_j P_k = \delta_{jk}P_k,\quad P_k^\dagger = P_k,\quad \sum_k P_k = \mathbb 1, \]

where \(P_k\) projects onto the whole eigenspace of eigenvalue \(a_k\). If \(a_k\) is non-degenerate, \(P_k=|a_k\rangle\langle a_k|\) has rank 1 and everything is easy. If \(a_k\) is degenerate, \(P_k\) has rank \(\ge 2\) — and now "collapse onto the eigenstate" is ambiguous, because there is a whole subspace of them. Von Neumann's original recipe resolved this by secretly picking a basis inside each eigenspace, so his post-measurement state depended on that arbitrary choice. Lüders' rule removes the arbitrariness by using only the projector \(P_k\), which the observable \(A\) actually determines.

What the rule says

For a system in state \(\rho\) (a density operator), measuring \(A\):

Selective — you read the outcome \(a_k\)

The outcome \(a_k\) occurs with the Born probability, and the state updates to the (renormalized) projected state:

\[ p_k = \mathrm{Tr}(P_k\,\rho), \qquad \rho \;\longrightarrow\; \rho_k = \frac{P_k\,\rho\,P_k}{\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\,\rho)}. \]

For a pure state this is the familiar \( |\psi\rangle \to P_k|\psi\rangle/\big\|P_k|\psi\rangle\big\| \).

Non-selective — you measure but don't (or can't) read the result

Averaging over outcomes with their probabilities gives

\[ \rho \;\longrightarrow\; \rho' = \sum_k p_k\,\rho_k = \sum_k P_k\,\rho\,P_k. \]

The one place it differs from von Neumann

For a non-degenerate \(A\), \(\sum_k P_k\rho P_k\) is just full decoherence in the eigenbasis — Lüders and von Neumann agree. For a degenerate \(A\) they split: Lüders' \(\sum_k P_k\rho P_k\) kills the coherences between different eigenvalues but preserves every coherence inside a single eigenspace; von Neumann's basis-refined rule dephases within the eigenspace too, destroying superpositions the measurement of \(A\) had no power to distinguish. A superposition of two states sharing the same eigenvalue \(a_k\) survives a Lüders measurement of \(A\) untouched.

How it is justified — three levels

1. It is the minimally disturbing update (repeatability + non-disturbance)

Lüders' rule is singled out by two physical demands beyond the Born rule.

Repeatability. Measure \(A\) again immediately: the outcome \(a_k\) recurs with certainty. Check it —

\[ \mathrm{Tr}(P_k\,\rho_k) = \frac{\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\,P_k\rho P_k)}{\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\rho)} = \frac{\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\rho)}{\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\rho)} = 1, \]

using \(P_k^2=P_k\) and cyclicity of the trace. A measurement that didn't do this couldn't be called a measurement of \(A\).

Non-disturbance (Lüders' theorem). The non-selective map disturbs exactly the observables incompatible with \(A\), and nothing more. Concretely, an observable \(B\) is left statistically untouched — \(\sum_k P_k B P_k = B\) — if and only if \([B,P_k]=0\) for all \(k\) (i.e. \(B\) is compatible with \(A\)). The easy direction is immediate: if \(P_kB=BP_k\), then

\[ \sum_k P_k B P_k = \sum_k B\,P_k P_k = B\sum_k P_k = B. \]

The converse — that no other update short of Lüders' leaves all compatible observables alone — is Lüders' 1951 result, and it is what pins the rule down uniquely. In words: the Lüders update is the gentlest change consistent with having learned the value of \(A\).

2. It is derived from unitary quantum mechanics (the measurement model)

Lüders' rule is not an independent mystery bolted onto the theory; it follows from an ordinary unitary interaction with a pointer plus the Born rule applied to that pointer. Couple the system to an apparatus, initially \(|0\rangle_A\), by the ideal unitary

\[ U = \sum_k P_k \otimes V_k, \qquad V_k|0\rangle_A = |k\rangle_A \ \text{(orthonormal pointer states)}, \]

with each \(V_k\) unitary — one checks \(U^\dagger U=\sum_k P_k\otimes \mathbb 1=\mathbb 1\), so \(U\) is unitary. Let it act:

\[ U\big(\rho\otimes|0\rangle\langle0|\big)U^\dagger = \sum_{k,k'} \big(P_k\,\rho\,P_{k'}\big)\otimes |k\rangle\langle k'|. \]

Now two moves recover both forms of the rule:

  • Ignore the pointer (trace it out). Since \(\langle k'|k\rangle=\delta_{kk'}\), the off-diagonal apparatus terms vanish and \(\mathrm{Tr}_A\) leaves \(\displaystyle\sum_k P_k\rho P_k\) — the non-selective Lüders map.
  • Read the pointer and find it at position \(k\) (an ordinary projective measurement of the apparatus): the system is left in \(P_k\rho P_k/\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\rho)\) with probability \(\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\rho)\) — the selective Lüders update.

The whole content of Lüders' rule is packed into the form of \(U\): because it couples through the eigenprojectors \(P_k\) — not through a finer basis inside each eigenspace — the apparatus records which eigenvalue and no more. Choose instead a coupling \(U_{\text{vN}}=\sum_{k,i}|k,i\rangle\langle k,i|\otimes W_{k,i}\) that also resolves a basis \(\{|k,i\rangle\}\) within the eigenspaces, and you get von Neumann's rule — but that is physically a different, finer measurement, and its answer depends on the basis. Lüders' rule is what you get by measuring \(A\) and only \(A\).

This is the same machinery as lesson 0001. The non-selective map \(\sum_k P_k\rho P_k\) is exactly decoherence in the eigenbasis of \(A\) (the improper mixture), and "read the pointer" is the selective collapse. Lüders' rule is the measurement-update postulate written out at the level of eigenspaces; the derivation here is just the ideal-measurement model of that lesson, specialized.

3. It is the right conditional state

Read information-theoretically, \(\rho_k = P_k\rho P_k/\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\rho)\) is the quantum analogue of Bayesian conditioning: it is the update that incorporates the fact "\(A=a_k\)" while changing the state as little as possible (it maximises fidelity to \(\rho\) among states supported in the eigenspace). Learn the least-committal thing the outcome tells you, and disturb the rest not at all.

How it is verified experimentally

Three layers of evidence, from the coarse structure down to the sharp Lüders-vs-von-Neumann distinction.

  • Repeatability and QND measurements. Quantum non-demolition setups (trapped ions, superconducting circuits, cavity QED) show that re-measuring an observable returns the same value — the repeatability built into the rule — and leave the projected state intact for further use.
  • The quantum Zeno effect and Zeno dynamics. Misra & Sudarshan (1977) showed that frequent Lüders projections freeze a system's evolution; this was seen by Itano et al. (1990) in trapped ions and since in every platform. The sharper version — projecting onto a degenerate subspace and watching coherent evolution continue inside it (quantum Zeno dynamics) — was realised with a Bose–Einstein condensate by Schäfer et al. (2014). That the confined dynamics stays coherent is precisely Lüders' "coherence within the eigenspace survives," not von Neumann's dephasing.
  • A direct discrimination. Hegerfeldt & Mayato (2012) designed a protocol whose statistics differ depending on which rule governs a degenerate measurement. An NMR implementation, "Discriminating between Lüders and von Neumann measuring devices" (2016), ran it and found the data favour Lüders' rule — the intra-eigenspace coherence is preserved, as Lüders predicts and von Neumann does not.

The one idea

After measuring \(A=\sum_k a_k P_k\), the state becomes \(\rho\to P_k\rho P_k/\mathrm{Tr}(P_k\rho)\) if you read \(a_k\), and \(\rho\to\sum_k P_k\rho P_k\) if you don't. Built from the eigenprojectors, this is the minimally disturbing update: it kills coherences between eigenvalues (which the measurement can tell apart) and preserves everything within an eigenspace (which it cannot). It is repeatable, it is derivable from a unitary pointer coupling plus the Born rule, and — for degenerate observables, where it genuinely differs from von Neumann's rule — it is the one experiment confirms.
