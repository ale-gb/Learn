Decoherence & the Measurement Problem

Why interacting with a big object doesn't collapse a wavefunction — how entanglement plus an inaccessible environment fakes collapse perfectly — and why, despite all that, the measurement postulate refuses to go away.

The question that starts it all

People say "the observer" is central to quantum mechanics, and hint that consciousness collapses the wavefunction. So sharpen it into an experiment: put a system in superposition, let it interact with a mindless macroscopic object, evolve, and only later measure with a real apparatus. Does the macroscopic interaction already collapse the state?

This is Wigner's friend with the friend replaced by a rock. Answering it forces us to be precise about what "measurement" even is — and the answer dissolves the role of consciousness entirely.

Step 1 — "Measurement" is just entanglement

Take a two-level system \( |\psi\rangle_S = c_0|0\rangle + c_1|1\rangle \) and a macroscopic apparatus/environment starting in \( |E_{\text{init}}\rangle \). A measurement interaction is a perfectly ordinary unitary evolution that correlates them (a von Neumann "pre-measurement"): whatever state the system is in, the environment records it.

By linearity of the Schrödinger equation, the joint state becomes entangled:

\[ |\Psi_{SE}\rangle = c_0\,|0\rangle\otimes|E_0\rangle \;+\; c_1\,|1\rangle\otimes|E_1\rangle \]

Notice what has not happened: nothing collapsed. \( |\Psi_{SE}\rangle \) is still a pure state evolving unitarily. The whole "measurement" is just the system getting entangled with a big thing.

Step 2 — The density matrix, and where coherence lives

A state vector only describes pure states — maximal knowledge. To carry classical uncertainty (statistical ignorance) alongside quantum superposition, use the density operator \( \rho = \sum_i p_i |\psi_i\rangle\langle\psi_i| \). For our system alone, if it were still pure,

\[ \rho_S = \begin{pmatrix} |c_0|^2 & c_0 c_1^{*} \\[2pt] c_1 c_0^{*} & |c_1|^2 \end{pmatrix}. \]

The diagonal entries are classical probabilities of \( |0\rangle \) or \( |1\rangle \). The off-diagonal entries are the coherences — they are what makes this a superposition rather than a coin flip; they are responsible for interference.

Handy facts: \( \mathrm{Tr}\,\rho = 1 \); \( \rho = \rho^\dagger \); \( \rho \succeq 0 \) (no negative probabilities); expectation values are \( \langle \hat O\rangle = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho\hat O) \). And the purity test: \( \mathrm{Tr}(\rho^2)=1 \) for a pure state, \( \lt 1 \) for a mixed one. That number dropping below 1 is loss of coherence, quantified.

Step 3 — Why you are forced to trace out the environment

You only have access to \( S \); you cannot measure the \( 10^{23} \) degrees of freedom of the environment \( E \). To predict any local measurement of an observable \( \hat O_A \) you must promote it to \( \hat O_A \otimes \hat I_B \) on the whole space, and demand a local \( \rho_A \) reproducing the true statistics:

\[ \mathrm{Tr}_A\!\big(\rho_A \hat O_A\big) \;=\; \mathrm{Tr}_{AB}\!\big(\rho_{AB}\,(\hat O_A \otimes \hat I_B)\big) \quad\text{for all } \hat O_A. \]

Expanding the right side in a product basis and collecting terms, there is a unique operator that satisfies this — the partial trace:

\[ \rho_A = \mathrm{Tr}_B(\rho_{AB}) \equiv \sum_j \langle b_j|\,\rho_{AB}\,|b_j\rangle. \]

So the partial trace is not a recipe someone chose; it's forced by the demand that local measurements come out right. Physically: "if I am blind to \(B\), what is the effective state of \(A\)?" If \(A\) and \(B\) are entangled, ignoring \(B\) averages over its outcomes — and that averaging launders quantum entanglement into classical ignorance about \(A\). This is exactly why a pure entangled \( \rho_{AB} \) yields a mixed \( \rho_A \).

Step 4 — Coherence dies exponentially

Trace the environment out of our entangled \( |\Psi_{SE}\rangle \). The diagonal survives untouched; the off-diagonals come back multiplied by the overlap of the environment states:

\[ \rho_S = |c_0|^2|0\rangle\langle0| + |c_1|^2|1\rangle\langle1| \;+\; c_0 c_1^{*}\,\langle E_1|E_0\rangle\,|0\rangle\langle1| \;+\; \text{h.c.} \]

Now, the environment is really \(N\) little pieces each scattering off the system, so its overlap factorizes:

\[ \langle E_1|E_0\rangle = \prod_{i=1}^{N}\langle e_{1,i}|e_{0,i}\rangle \sim (1-\epsilon)^{N} \xrightarrow[\;N\ \text{large}\;]{} 0. \]

Each piece is only slightly disturbed (\(1-\epsilon\)), but raised to Avogadro-scale \(N\) the product collapses to zero in a fraction of a nanosecond. The reduced state becomes diagonal:

\[ \rho_S \approx \begin{pmatrix} |c_0|^2 & 0 \\ 0 & |c_1|^2 \end{pmatrix}. \]

The one idea

Nothing collapsed. The global state is still a pure superposition — the quantum information didn't vanish, it leaked into correlations with the environment you can't access. Trace that environment out and, locally, what's left is mathematically identical to a classical mixture in which a collapse "happened." Decoherence is the appearance of collapse, manufactured by ignorance of the environment.

Which basis survives (position, not weird superpositions of it) is itself explained: the environment "monitors" certain observables, einselecting robust pointer states — environment-induced superselection (Zurek). That's why the classical world looks like it does.

Step 5 — You cannot beat it downstream (your "disjoint supports" idea)

Tempting escape: after the first interaction, evolve the system cleverly so that a genuine early collapse would send it somewhere a surviving superposition wouldn't — engineer the final distributions to have disjoint supports, so a single click tells you what happened.

It cannot work, and the reason is linearity. Whether collapse or decoherence occurred, the local state right after the interaction is the same matrix:

\[ \rho_{\text{collapse}} = p_1|1\rangle\langle1| + p_2|2\rangle\langle2| \;=\; \rho_{\text{decohered}}. \]

Any physical evolution is a completely-positive trace-preserving map \( \mathcal{E} \), and every such map is linear:

\[ \mathcal{E}\big(p_1\rho_1 + p_2\rho_2\big) = p_1\,\mathcal{E}(\rho_1) + p_2\,\mathcal{E}(\rho_2). \]

Identical inputs give identical outputs. Even if \( |1\rangle\to|\phi_A\rangle \) and \( |2\rangle\to|\phi_B\rangle \) land in disjoint detectors, both stories predict "\(A\) with probability \(p_1\), \(B\) with \(p_2\)" — and a single click at \(A\) can't tell you whether it was \(|1\rangle\) all along or was only "realized" as \(A\) at the final measurement. The only way to tell them apart is to stop tracing out the environment and measure the \(S\)–\(E\) correlations directly (that's what the reversal, Wigner's-friend, and Leggett–Garg experiments below do).

Step 6 — So why keep the measurement postulate at all?

If decoherence turns superpositions into classical-looking mixtures, why not drop "collapse" and keep unitary evolution everywhere? Because decoherence solves a lot, but leaves one thing untouched.

Decoherence does explainDecoherence does not explain
Why interference is lost (off-diagonals vanish deterministically)Why you get one definite outcome — the detector clicks once
Which basis becomes classical (einselection / pointer states)Why this branch is realized and the other isn't
The quantum→classical transition at the level of ensemblesSingle events — nothing in linear evolution deletes a branch

The reduced \( \rho_S \) describes an ensemble: run it many times and you'll get \(|c_1|^2\), \(|c_2|^2\). But a single run gives a single fact. Strict unitarity keeps the whole universe in \( c_1|1\rangle|E_1\rangle + c_2|2\rangle|E_2\rangle \) forever — decoherence stops the branches interfering but never annihilates one. To get from "branches don't interfere" to "only one branch is in my lab," you must either:

  • Add the measurement postulate (Copenhagen): a pragmatic patch — apply the Born rule, pick one outcome, update. An admission the theory is silent on single events.
  • Accept Many-Worlds (Everett): refuse the patch, keep only unitarity, and both outcomes are real — experienced by decohered, orthogonal copies of you.

There's even a consistency reason you can't simply "derive collapse from decoherence": the partial-trace argument already uses the Born rule (\( \langle\hat O\rangle = \mathrm{Tr}(\rho\hat O)\)) to justify tracing out. Using a Born-rule tool to then derive the Born rule is circular. (Zurek's envariance program tries to break this circle from entanglement symmetry — elegant, still debated.)

Worked example — why the double-slit screen doesn't collapse the electron

Apply the machinery to the most familiar setup. As the electron reaches the barrier its wavefunction has three parts: the bit that hits the solid wall, and the bits through the left and right slits.

The wall decoheres; the slits don't. The part that hits the wall scatters inelastically off the lattice — environment states diverge, \( \langle E_{\text{hit}}|E_{\text{miss}}\rangle \to 0 \), that portion is "measured" and stops. But the part passing the slits barely touches anything. A massive, rigid screen recoils by less than its own quantum/thermal jitter, so the screen state is the same whichever slit the electron took:

\[ |E_L\rangle \approx |E_R\rangle \quad\Longrightarrow\quad \langle E_R|E_L\rangle \approx 1. \]

The overlap that multiplies the coherences is \(\approx 1\), so the off-diagonal terms survive. No which-path information leaked; no decoherence.

Making it exact: discretize space

Put the electron on a lattice, \( |\psi\rangle_S = \sum_i c_i|x_i\rangle \), with wall sites \(X_{\text{wall}}\) (each giving an orthogonal, distinct recoil \( \langle E_i|E_j\rangle\approx\delta_{ij}\)) and open slits \(x_L, x_R\) (leaving the screen in its ground state \(|E_0\rangle\)). The entangled output is

\[ |\Psi_{\text{out}}\rangle = \sum_{x_i\in X_{\text{wall}}} c_i|x_i\rangle\otimes|E_i\rangle \;+\; c_L|x_L\rangle\otimes|E_0\rangle \;+\; c_R|x_R\rangle\otimes|E_0\rangle. \]

Trace out the screen. Every wall cross-term dies (orthogonal recoils); the two slit terms share the same \(|E_0\rangle\) so their cross-term lives. The reduced state splits cleanly:

\[ \rho_S = \underbrace{\sum_{x_i\in X_{\text{wall}}} |c_i|^2\,|x_i\rangle\langle x_i|}_{\text{decohered: hit the wall}} \;+\; \underbrace{\big(c_L|x_L\rangle+c_R|x_R\rangle\big)\big(c_L^{*}\langle x_L|+c_R^{*}\langle x_R|\big)}_{\text{coherent: passed the slits}}. \]

The screen acted as a selective position-projector: it measured the electron everywhere except at the slits, and the surviving coherent piece is exactly what propagates on to interfere.

A subtlety you caught: a click on the far screen proves nothing by itself

Seeing one electron land at the back does not prove it stayed coherent — it only proves it wasn't absorbed by the wall. A state collapsed to \(|x_L\rangle\) is a sharp position, so by \( \Delta x\,\Delta p \gtrsim \hbar/2 \) it has huge momentum spread and fans out into a broad wave that also lands all over the back screen. The collapsed and coherent distributions overlap, so a single click is ambiguous. You need the ensemble.

Reading collapse off the fringes: visibility

Let a fraction \(f\) of electrons fully decohere and \(1-f\) stay coherent. The ensemble state carries a damped coherence \( \gamma = 1-f \):

\[ \rho_{\text{ens}} = (1-f)\,\rho_{\text{coh}} + f\,\rho_{\text{dec}} = \begin{pmatrix} \tfrac12 & \tfrac{1-f}{2}\\[2pt] \tfrac{1-f}{2} & \tfrac12\end{pmatrix}, \]

and propagating to the back screen gives

\[ P(y) \propto 2|\psi_0|^2\big(1 + \gamma\cos\Delta\phi\big), \qquad V \equiv \frac{I_{\max}-I_{\min}}{I_{\max}+I_{\min}} = \gamma = 1-f. \]

So — your deduction, made quantitative — a crisp pattern with dark nodes (\(I_{\min}\approx0\), \(V\approx1\)) means \(f\approx0\): almost none of the electrons collapsed at the slits. The visible fringes are the off-diagonal terms of the density matrix, made of light.

The real dividing line: information, not size

The punchline that overturns the naive Copenhagen picture: size does not cause collapse. Irreversible leakage of which-path information does. Two macroscopic interactions, opposite outcomes:

InteractionWhat happensCoherence?
Beam splitter / mirror (trillions of atoms)Elastic, coherent scattering off bulk modes; rigid recoil below the mirror's own uncertainty. \( \langle E_{\text{refl}}|E_{\text{trans}}\rangle\approx1 \)Survives — this is how we build superpositions
CCD pixel / retinaInelastic absorption → irreversible macroscopic avalanche. \( \langle E_{\text{click}}|E_{\text{no click}}\rangle\approx0 \)Destroyed — genuine decoherence

This is why quantum computing is even possible: qubits are steered by macroscopic lasers, cables, and fields all day. What matters is keeping those interactions elastic and reversible so no which-path record is left behind.

How the actual experiments peer behind the veil

Since local operations can't distinguish collapse from decoherence, experiments enlarge the "system" to include the macroscopic object and refuse to let information escape to an uncontrolled environment — then probe the global coherence three ways:

StrategyCollapse predictsUnitary+decoherence predictsResult
Coherent reversal (apply \( \hat U^\dagger \))Irreversible — phase goneRecover the pristine superpositionReversal achieved (O'Connell 2010, a naked-eye mechanical drum)
Wigner's friend (rotated / Bell-basis on the "friend")Mixed state, no cross-termsInterference from the off-diagonalsCoherence confirmed (Proietti 2019, Bong 2020)
Leggett–Garg \( K_3 = C_{12}+C_{23}-C_{13} \)\( K_3 \le 1 \) (macrorealism)\( K_3 \) up to \( 1.5 \)\( K_3 > 1 \) for ever-larger objects

Every result to date: no mass threshold where Schrödinger evolution spontaneously fails. Consciousness is nowhere in the story — a "measurement" is just entanglement with enough inaccessible degrees of freedom to make the leak irreversible. (Objective-collapse theories like Penrose's gravitational proposal predict a real threshold and are still being tested.)
