Lesson 0002 · Decoherence & Classicality

Einselection: Why the World Chooses Position

Decoherence kills superpositions — but superpositions of what? Something must pick out the special basis that survives. This lesson is the answer: the environment selects the states it can watch without disturbing, and because forces depend on position, those states are localized. That selection is where the classical world comes from.

Why this lesson

In lesson 0001 we said the reduced density matrix "goes diagonal." You may have felt a splinter: diagonal in which basis? A matrix is only diagonal relative to a choice of basis — so "decoherence diagonalizes \(\rho\)" is not yet a physical statement. We owe you the missing ingredient: the dynamical rule that singles out one preferred basis — the pointer basis — and the reason it turns out to be position. This is einselection (environment-induced superselection), and it's the step that turns decoherence from bookkeeping into an explanation of the classical world.

The splinter: diagonality is basis-dependent

Take the decohered state from last time, \( \rho_S = \tfrac12|0\rangle\langle0| + \tfrac12|1\rangle\langle1| \). It looks like a nice classical mixture of \(|0\rangle\) and \(|1\rangle\). But rewrite it in the rotated basis \( |\pm\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}(|0\rangle\pm|1\rangle) \) and you get \( \rho_S = \tfrac12|+\rangle\langle+| + \tfrac12|-\rangle\langle-| \) — an equally valid "classical mixture," now of \(|+\rangle\) and \(|-\rangle\). The maximally mixed matrix is diagonal in every basis at once.

So "the off-diagonal terms vanished" cannot be the whole story: which entries are "off-diagonal" depends on the basis you wrote \(\rho\) in. Worse, any density matrix is diagonal in its own eigenbasis (the Schmidt basis of the system–environment state), so you can always find a basis in which "collapse happened." If the theory doesn't tell us which basis is the physical, stable, classical one, it predicts nothing. This is the preferred-basis problem, and it is the real content of "why position."

Pointer states are the robust states

The resolution is dynamical, and it's due to Zurek. Don't ask which basis \(\rho\) happens to be diagonal in at one instant; ask which states are stable — which ones the environment can monitor without kicking the system around, so that they persist instead of smearing into superpositions.

Model the coupling as an interaction Hamiltonian in which the environment reads off some system observable \(\hat A\):

\[ \hat H_{\text{int}} = \hat A_S \otimes \hat B_E . \]

Feed in an eigenstate of \(\hat A\), say \(\hat A|a\rangle = a|a\rangle\), with the environment in \(|E_0\rangle\). The interaction gives

\[ \hat H_{\text{int}}\,\big(|a\rangle\otimes|E_0\rangle\big) = a\,|a\rangle \otimes \hat B|E_0\rangle . \]

The system stays put in \(|a\rangle\); only the environment evolves (conditioned on \(a\)). The state \(|a\rangle\) is robust — it gets recorded by the environment but is never disturbed by it. A superposition \(\sum_a c_a|a\rangle\), by contrast, becomes entangled with distinct environment states and decoheres. So the surviving, classical basis is the eigenbasis of \(\hat A\).

The one idea

The pointer states are the ones the environment monitors without perturbing — formally, the eigenstates of the observable \(\hat\Pi\) that commutes with the interaction Hamiltonian, \([\hat\Pi,\hat H_{\text{int}}]=0\) (Zurek's commutativity criterion). They alone survive; every superposition of them is einselected away. The classical basis isn't put in by hand — it's chosen by what the environment couples to.

Why that observable is position

Now the physics answer to "why not momentum, or energy, or some weird basis." It comes down to a single empirical fact about the interactions that dominate everyday environments:

Real environments — photons, air molecules, phonons — scatter off an object according to where it is. Forces are functions of position.

That means the coupling operator \(\hat A\) is (almost always) a function of position, \(\hat A = f(\hat x)\). The environment is, physically, a position monitor: it continually measures where things are. So the einselected pointer states are the (approximate) eigenstates of position — localized wave packets. A superposition of an object "here" and "there" — a Schrödinger-cat state in position — is exactly what the environment reads out fastest, so it decoheres almost instantly. A single localized packet is left alone.

This is the same fact you already met wearing a different hat. In the double slit, "which-path information" leaking to the environment was position monitoring; the rigid screen preserved coherence precisely because it failed to resolve the electron's position at the slits. Which-path decoherence and einselection of position are one phenomenon.

How fast? For a superposition of two locations a distance \(\Delta x\) apart in a thermal environment, the decoherence time is shorter than the ordinary dynamical (relaxation) time by a factor \( \big(\lambda_{\text{dB}}/\Delta x\big)^2 \), where \(\lambda_{\text{dB}}\) is the thermal de Broglie wavelength — often \( \sim 10^{-40} \) for a dust grain separated by a hair's width. Position superpositions don't merely decohere; they decohere overwhelmingly faster the more macroscopically separated they are. That steepness is why big things are never caught mid-superposition.

The refinement: coherent states, not sharp positions

Exact position eigenstates can't be the final answer, and the reason is the system's own dynamics. A perfectly sharp \(|x\rangle\) has infinite momentum spread (\(\Delta x\,\Delta p \ge \hbar/2\)), so the free Hamiltonian \( \hat H_S = \hat p^2/2m \) blows it apart instantly — it is not robust at all. The true pointer states are a compromise between two competing bosses: the interaction \(\hat H_{\text{int}}\) wants sharp position; the self-Hamiltonian \(\hat H_S\) wants to not spread. The winners are minimum-uncertainty Gaussian wave packets — approximate coherent states — localized in both position and momentum, i.e. localized in phase space.

Zurek makes this quantitative with the predictability sieve: take every candidate pure initial state, let it evolve under the full system-plus-environment dynamics, and measure how fast it loses purity — how quickly its entropy \( S = -\mathrm{Tr}(\rho\ln\rho) \) grows as it entangles with the environment. Sift out everything except the states that stay pure longest, that generate the least entanglement. For a particle whose environment monitors position, the sieve's survivors are exactly those Gaussian coherent states. They are the most predictable states — the ones whose future the environment barely disturbs.

This is where classicality comes from

Einselection hands you localized wave packets that stay localized and, by Ehrenfest's theorem, ride along Newtonian trajectories \( \langle\hat x\rangle(t) \). A world made of objects with reasonably definite positions and momenta — points moving through phase space — is not assumed; it is selected, continuously, by the environment monitoring position. Classical mechanics is the physics of the pointer states our environment leaves alone.

A crucial caveat: "position" is contingent, not a law

The rule is "eigenstates of whatever the environment monitors" — and that is usually position only because scattering forces are position-dependent. Change the coupling and you change the pointer basis. When the self-Hamiltonian dominates over a weak environment and the system's energy is effectively conserved, the pointer states become energy eigenstates instead (einselection of energy — relevant for, e.g., a mode in a cavity, or quantum-optical damping). For a superconducting loop the pointer observable is magnetic flux; for a spin coupled through \(\hat\sigma_z\), it's \(\hat\sigma_z\). So the everyday dominance of position is a fact about our interactions, not a metaphysical law — a genuinely deep and slightly humbling point. The environment doesn't privilege position; our environment happens to measure it, everywhere, all the time.
