Why Quantum Mechanics Needs Complex Numbers

A single spin-½ already forces the issue. But the deep reason isn't "states need a phase" — it's that one object, the complex structure \(i\), is what locks together the three things quantum mechanics must satisfy at once: probability, dynamics, and composition.

Why this lesson

"Why complex numbers?" has answers at three depths, and this lesson climbs all three. (I) A concrete no-go: one spin-½, à la Stern–Gerlach, cannot be modelled over \(\mathbb R^2\). (II) The subtle objection — "isn't \(\mathbb C^N\) just \(\mathbb R^{2N}\)?" — and why the answer is no, from Stone's theorem, the commutator, and the composition of systems (with a real experiment). (III) The deepest view: \(i\) is not assumed but emerges from the real algebra of observables, and the Hilbert space with it.

Part I — One spin-½ already breaks \(\mathbb R^2\)

The empirical input is minimal. A silver atom's spin can be measured along any spatial axis; along each axis the result is \(\pm\tfrac{\hbar}{2}\); and for any two orthogonal axes, a state sharp along one gives \(50/50\) along the other. The three axes \(x,y,z\) are, in quantum-information language, mutually unbiased: knowing the outcome in one basis leaves you maximally ignorant in the others.

Try to build it over \(\mathbb R^2\)

Take the \(S_z\) eigenstates as the canonical basis, \(|{+}z\rangle=\binom{1}{0}\), \(|{-}z\rangle=\binom{0}{1}\). The \(S_x\) eigenstates must be \(50/50\) in the \(z\)-basis — equal-magnitude components — so in \(\mathbb R^2\) they are forced (up to sign) to be

\[ |{\pm}x\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\binom{1}{\pm 1}. \]

Now demand a third basis, \(S_y\), unbiased to both. Write \(|{+}y\rangle=\binom{\cos\theta}{\sin\theta}\). Unbiasedness to \(z\) requires \(\cos^2\theta=\tfrac12\), forcing \(\theta\in\{\tfrac{\pi}{4},\tfrac{3\pi}{4},\dots\}\) — but those are exactly the \(x\)-eigenvectors again. So any real vector unbiased to \(z\) is already an \(x\)-eigenstate, hence perfectly biased (\(100/0\)) with respect to \(x\), not \(50/50\). There is no room in \(\mathbb R^2\) for a third mutually unbiased basis.

This is a counting fact: \(\mathbb R^2\) admits at most two mutually unbiased bases (the two diagonals of a square), while three spatial axes demand three. Complexifying to \(\mathbb C^2\) opens exactly the missing room — the state space becomes the Bloch sphere \(S^2\), which carries three MUBs — and the third basis is

\[ |{\pm}y\rangle = \tfrac{1}{\sqrt2}\binom{1}{\pm i}, \qquad |\langle {+}x|{+}y\rangle|^2 = \Big|\tfrac12(1+i)\Big|^2 = \tfrac12,\quad |\langle{+}z|{+}y\rangle|^2 = \tfrac12. \]

The imaginary component is precisely what makes \(|{+}y\rangle\) unbiased to both \(z\) and \(x\) at once.

The same fact, said with operators

Over a real Hilbert space, "self-adjoint" means "real symmetric." The real symmetric \(2\times2\) matrices form a 3-dimensional space, spanned by

\[ \mathbb 1=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&1\end{pmatrix},\quad \sigma_z=\begin{pmatrix}1&0\\0&-1\end{pmatrix},\quad \sigma_x=\begin{pmatrix}0&1\\1&0\end{pmatrix}, \]

so there are only two independent traceless ones. But spin-½ needs three traceless observables \(S_x,S_y,S_z\) closing into the \(\mathfrak{su}(2)\) algebra. The third, \(\sigma_y=\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&-i\\ i&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)\), is Hermitian but not real symmetric; its real cousin \(\bigl(\begin{smallmatrix}0&-1\\ 1&0\end{smallmatrix}\bigr)=-i\sigma_y\) is antisymmetric, and real antisymmetric matrices have purely imaginary eigenvalues \(\pm i\). Since measured values are real, an antisymmetric operator cannot be an observable. Real symmetric matrices simply cannot carry the spin algebra — you are forced to complex Hermitian matrices.

The same pressure shows up in the canonical commutator. For Hermitian \(A,B\), the commutator is anti-Hermitian: \([A,B]^\dagger = B^\dagger A^\dagger - A^\dagger B^\dagger = -[A,B]\). So \([\hat x,\hat p]\) must be anti-Hermitian — yet it equals a multiple of the identity, which is Hermitian. The only escape is to make that multiple imaginary: \([\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar\,\mathbb 1\). The \(i\) is not decoration; it is what reconciles the two.

Part II — "But isn't \(\mathbb C^N\) just \(\mathbb R^{2N}\)?"

Here is the objection a careful reader raises, and it is the right one. A complex amplitude \(c=a+ib\) is a pair of reals \(\binom{a}{b}\); a complex Hilbert space \(\mathbb C^N\) is, as a real vector space, just \(\mathbb R^{2N}\). If "a state needs a magnitude and a phase" were the whole story, you could track the phase with an extra real coordinate and never write an \(i\). So the intuition "\(i\) keeps track of phase, like 3D vectors keep track of force directions" undersells it. The real content is that \(\mathbb C^N\) is \(\mathbb R^{2N}\) plus one extra piece of structure — the operation "multiply by \(i\)," a real-linear map \(J\) with \(J^2=-\mathbb 1\) — and that \(J\) is forced to be compatible with three things simultaneously. That triple compatibility is what \(\mathbb R^{2N}\) alone has no reason to provide.

(a) Observables must also be generators — Stone's theorem

In quantum mechanics observables lead a double life: they are what you measure, and they are the generators of continuous symmetries — momentum generates translations, the Hamiltonian generates time evolution, angular momentum generates rotations. A one-parameter group of probability-preserving maps \(U(\theta)\) is, by Stone's theorem, \(U(\theta)=e^{\theta G}\) with an anti-Hermitian generator, \(G^\dagger=-G\) (that is exactly the condition making \(U\) unitary).

Now the clash. A measured quantity needs real eigenvalues (Hermitian). A generator needs to be anti-Hermitian. Over the reals these are irreconcilable: a real skew-symmetric \(G\) (\(G^{\mathsf T}=-G\)) has purely imaginary eigenvalues, so in a real Hilbert space the generator of a symmetry cannot be an observable at all. The imaginary unit is precisely the bridge — it maps one class onto the other:

\[ G = -\,\tfrac{i}{\hbar}\,A, \qquad G^\dagger=-G \iff A^\dagger = A. \]

So \(i\) is the isomorphism that lets the very same object be measured (as \(A\), Hermitian) and drive dynamics (as \(G=-iA/\hbar\), anti-Hermitian). Strip out \(i\) and you must give up either real spectra or unitary dynamics.

(b) The commutator carries a symplectic structure

Quantisation turns the classical Poisson bracket into the commutator, \(\{\,\cdot\,,\cdot\,\}\to\frac{1}{i\hbar}[\,\cdot\,,\cdot\,]\). We just saw \([\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar\,\mathbb 1\) cannot be written over \(\mathbb R\): the commutator of two symmetric operators is antisymmetric, yet the right side is a (symmetric) multiple of the identity. Geometrically this is telling you something: the quantum state space is not just a metric space (an inner product measuring probabilities) — it also carries a symplectic form (the antisymmetric structure governing Hamiltonian flow), exactly as classical phase space does. The complex structure \(J\) (=multiplication by \(i\)) is what makes the metric \(g\) and the symplectic form \(\omega\) compatible, via \(g(u,v)=\omega(u,Jv)\). A space with all three fitting together is a Kähler space; the projective space of quantum states \(\mathbb{CP}^{N-1}\) is one. \(i\) is the glue of that compatibility, not an accountant's shortcut.

(c) Systems must compose — local tomography

The sharpest argument comes from putting two systems together. A basic demand — local tomography — is that the full state of a bipartite system \(AB\) (entanglement included) be fixed by the statistics of local measurements and their correlations, \(\langle O_A\otimes O_B\rangle\). Count the observables for two qubits:

Complex \(\mathbb C^2\otimes\mathbb C^2\)Real \(\mathbb R^2\otimes\mathbb R^2\)
Global observables (dim)\((d_Ad_B)^2 = 16\)\(\tfrac{d_Ad_B(d_Ad_B+1)}{2}=10\)
Spanned by local products\(d_A^2\,d_B^2 = 4\times4 = 16\)\(3\times 3 = 9\)

Over \(\mathbb C\) the counts match exactly: local products of observables span all global observables, so joint local statistics determine everything. Over \(\mathbb R\) they don't — and you can name the missing observable. In the Pauli basis, \(\sigma_i\otimes\sigma_j\) is a real matrix precisely when both factors are real (\(\mathbb 1,\sigma_x,\sigma_z\)) or both are imaginary (\(\sigma_y\)). That gives \(3\times3=9\) products of local real observables, plus one extra: \(\sigma_y\otimes\sigma_y\), which is a legitimate real symmetric global observable but is not a product of local real ones (since \(\sigma_y\) alone isn't a real observable). So real quantum mechanics hides a global correlation that no amount of local measurement can reveal — you would need to measure the whole system at once. Complex quantum mechanics has exactly the right amount of structure for local tomography to hold.

This one was actually tested

This is not only philosophy. Renou et al. (Nature, 2021) designed a Bell-like network experiment in which — under the standard assumption that independent sources combine by tensor product — real and complex quantum mechanics make different, measurable predictions; superconducting-qubit and photonic experiments then confirmed the complex prediction, falsifying real quantum theory under those assumptions. The honest caveat: that assumption is essentially local tomography, and one can still build a real formulation that reproduces everything by carrying an extra real operator \(J\) with \(J^2=-\mathbb 1\) commuting with all observables (Stueckelberg, 1960) — but that operator is simply \(i\) in disguise. Which is the real lesson of Part II: you can hide the complex structure inside \(\mathbb R^{2N}\), but you cannot eliminate it.

Part III — Where \(i\) actually comes from: the algebra of observables

Parts I–II show \(\mathbb C\) is needed. The deepest treatment shows it is not even an input — it emerges from the real structure of measurable quantities. This reverses the usual pedagogy, which starts by postulating a complex Hilbert space.

Start with only what you can measure

Let \(\mathcal A_{\mathbb R}\) be the set of observables — a real vector space (you can scale an observable by a real number and add observables). It carries two natural bilinear products:

  • a Jordan product \(A\circ B\) — symmetric and commutative (you need to square observables to speak of variances), but not associative;
  • a Lie bracket \(\{A,B\}=-\{B,A\}\) — antisymmetric, obeying the Jacobi identity (observables generate transformations, and composing transformations needs a Lie bracket).

For these to describe one system, the generators must act as derivations on the products — the Leibniz rule \(\{A,\,B\circ C\}=\{A,B\}\circ C + B\circ\{A,C\}\). A real vector space with \(\circ\) and \(\{\,,\}\) obeying this is a Jordan–Lie algebra. There is no \(i\) anywhere in it.

Demand associativity, and \(i\) is born

Physics badly wants a single associative product \(AB\) (to compose measurements, form polynomials, build a \(C^*\)-algebra). The most general one built from the two we have is

\[ AB = A\circ B + \kappa\,\{A,B\}, \qquad \kappa\in\mathbb ? \]

Impose associativity, \((AB)C=A(BC)\). The terms linear in \(\kappa\) cancel identically — that is exactly what the Leibniz rule guarantees. What survives equates the non-associativity of the Jordan product to \(\kappa^2\) times a nested Lie bracket. Matching this against the Jordan–Lie structure, and demanding that states be positive (\(\omega(A^2)\ge 0\), so that a genuine probability interpretation and a \(C^*\)-norm exist), forces

\[ \kappa^2 = -\tfrac{\hbar^2}{4} \;<\;0 \qquad\Longrightarrow\qquad \kappa = \tfrac{i\hbar}{2}. \]

A real \(\kappa\) (the sign \(\kappa^2>0\)) gives a non-physical "split" algebra with no positive states; only the negative sign works, and it is imaginary. So the associative product is

\[ AB = A\circ B + \tfrac{i\hbar}{2}\,\{A,B\}, \]

with \(A\circ B=\tfrac12(AB+BA)\) and the Lie bracket represented by the Hermitian object \(\{A,B\}=\tfrac{1}{i\hbar}(AB-BA)\). Demanding associativity of the observables has complexified the algebra by itself: the real Jordan–Lie algebra of observables closes up into a complex \(C^*\)-algebra, with \(i\) generated, not assumed.

Contrast the classical case: there the Jordan product is ordinary (associative, commutative) multiplication of functions, the Lie bracket is the Poisson bracket, and the associativity condition is satisfied with \(\kappa=0\) — the two products stay separate and no \(i\) appears. Quantum mechanics is exactly the case where the observables fail to commute, forcing \(\kappa\neq0\), forcing \(\kappa\) imaginary.

The Hilbert space is not assumed either — it emerges (GNS)

Now we have an abstract complex \(C^*\)-algebra \(\mathcal A\) but still no kets. In this framework a state is not a vector; it is a positive linear functional \(\omega:\mathcal A\to\mathbb C\) with \(\omega(\mathbb 1)=1\) — it simply assigns to each observable its expectation value. The GNS construction (Gelfand–Naimark–Segal) then builds a Hilbert space out of the algebra and a state, in three moves:

  • Inner product from the state: \(\langle A,B\rangle_\omega := \omega(A^*B)\) — a sesquilinear, positive-semidefinite form on \(\mathcal A\).
  • Quotient the null directions: the "zero-length" elements \(\mathcal N_\omega=\{A:\omega(A^*A)=0\}\) form a subspace; pass to \(\mathcal A/\mathcal N_\omega\), on which the form is a genuine inner product.
  • Complete to get a bona fide complex Hilbert space \(\mathcal H_\omega\). The algebra then acts on it by left multiplication, \(\pi_\omega(A)\,[B]=[AB]\), recovering operators-on-a-Hilbert-space — as a theorem, not a postulate.

The one idea

Complex numbers are not bookkeeping for a phase — if they were, \(\mathbb R^{2N}\) would do. \(i\) is the complex structure \(J\) that forces the metric of probability, the symplectic form of dynamics, and the tensor product of composition to all fit together at once — which is why the same object can be measured and generate evolution, why \([\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar\), and why local measurements suffice to pin down a joint state. Algebraically, \(i\) isn't even assumed: demanding that the real algebra of observables be associative and positive generates it, and the Hilbert space then emerges from a state by GNS.

So what is the intuition?

Your instinct — "states carry a phase, and \(i\) tracks it, like 3D vectors track force directions" — is the right flavour but too weak, and the \(\mathbb R^{2N}\) objection is why. Extra components (a second real number per amplitude) you could always bolt on. What you cannot bolt on for free is the compatibility: the demand that the structure preserving probabilities (orthogonal/unitary transformations) be the same structure that generates dynamics (the symplectic/Hamiltonian flow), and that this survive composing systems. The complex unit \(i\) is exactly the object that identifies those structures — it turns "rotate the probability geometry" and "flow under a Hamiltonian" into two faces of one operation, just as, on the plane, multiplication by \(i\) fuses rotation and the metric into a single algebraic act. That fusion, not the mere existence of a phase, is what quantum mechanics cannot do without.
