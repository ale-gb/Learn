Where Observables Come From: Symmetry, and the Angular-Momentum Puzzle

What principle decides which operators are physical observables? The answer is symmetry: observables are the generators of the system's symmetry transformations. Angular momentum is the perfect test case â€” and it resolves your puzzle about components versus magnitude.

The question â€” with one spoiler correction

You asked why there are observables for the components of angular momentum but (seemingly) not for their magnitude. First, a correction that turns out to be the whole story: the magnitude-squared \(\hat J^2\) is an observable â€” but a very special one (the Casimir), and for the spin of a given particle it is a fixed label, not a variable. Underneath sits the deeper question â€” what determines the observables at all? â€” and the single answer to both is: the symmetry group of the system.

The gap in the textbook answer

Intro QM says: an observable is a self-adjoint operator (real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenbasis). True â€” but hopelessly non-selective. The self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space form a vast sea; almost none of them correspond to anything you would call a physical quantity. Self-adjointness is a necessary condition, not the source. Something must single out position, momentum, energy, angular momentum, spin, charge as the observables of nature. That something is symmetry.

Observables are the generators of symmetries

A continuous symmetry acts on states by a family of unitary operators \(U(s)\). By Stone's theorem every such family is generated by a self-adjoint operator \(\hat G\):

\[ U(s) = e^{-\,i\,s\,\hat G/\hbar}. \]

That generator \(\hat G\) is an observable â€” self-adjoint by construction â€” and (Noether, quantum version) it is conserved exactly when the symmetry commutes with the dynamics, \([\hat G,\hat H]=0\). So the fundamental observables are not chosen by hand; they are read off from the symmetry group the physics respects:

This is the modern, symmetry-first view of quantum mechanics (Wigner; developed cleanly by Ballentine): the whole list of "canonical" observables â€” \(\mathbf x,\mathbf p,\mathbf J,H\) â€” and even their commutation relations follow from projectively representing the spacetime symmetry group (Galilei non-relativistically, PoincarĂ© relativistically) on Hilbert space. You don't postulate \([\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar\); you derive it from the group.

Angular momentum = the generators of rotation

Apply this to rotations. A rotation by angle \(\theta\) about axis \(\hat{\mathbf n}\) acts as \(U=e^{-\,i\,\theta\,\hat{\mathbf n}\cdot\hat{\mathbf J}/\hbar}\), so there are three generators \(\hat J_x,\hat J_y,\hat J_z\) â€” one per rotation axis. Their algebra is not an assumption; it is forced by the geometry of rotations, which fail to commute (rotating about \(x\) then \(y\) differs from \(y\) then \(x\)):

\[ [\hat J_i,\hat J_j] = i\hbar\,\epsilon_{ijk}\,\hat J_k . \]

Each \(\hat J_i\) is self-adjoint, hence each is a bona fide observable. That is why the components are observables: they are the generators of rotations. There is nothing arbitrary about them â€” remove rotational symmetry and they would not exist as a distinguished triple.

Why you can't have all three components at once

Because the generators do not commute, they share no common eigenbasis: no state can be an eigenstate of \(\hat J_x\), \(\hat J_y\) and \(\hat J_z\) together (except the trivial \(\mathbf J=0\)). The algebra directly forces an uncertainty relation,

\[ \Delta J_x\,\Delta J_y \;\ge\; \tfrac{\hbar}{2}\,\big|\langle \hat J_z\rangle\big| , \]

so at most one component can be sharp â€” conventionally \(\hat J_z\). The full vector \(\hat{\mathbf J}\), with a simultaneously definite \(x\), \(y\) and \(z\), is simply not a measurable object. (Notice this "uncertainty" is a fact about the symmetry algebra, not about a clumsy measuring device.)

The magnitude is an observable â€” the Casimir

Now the magnitude. Form \(\hat J^2 = \hat J_x^2+\hat J_y^2+\hat J_z^2\). A short computation with the algebra above gives

\[ [\hat J^2,\hat J_i] = 0 \quad\text{for every } i . \]

\(\hat J^2\) commutes with all the generators â€” it is the Casimir invariant of the rotation algebra. So, unlike the individual components, \(\hat J^2\) can be measured together with one component. The standard complete set of commuting observables is \(\{\hat J^2,\hat J_z\}\), with the familiar spectrum

\[ \hat J^2 \to \hbar^2\,j(j+1), \qquad \hat J_z \to \hbar\,m,\quad m=-j,\dots,+j . \]

So the magnitude-squared is very much an observable. But being the Casimir gives it a completely different role from the components: it commutes with everything, so it is constant on each irreducible representation. It does not point in any direction; it labels which representation you are in â€” the value of \(j\).

The resolution: for spin, the magnitude is a fixed label

Here is the precise sense in which your intuition was right. Whether \(\hat J^2\) behaves as a "variable you measure" or a "fixed property" depends on whether the physical system can occupy different irreducible representations:

CaseCan \(j\) change?So \(\hat J^2\) isâ€¦
Orbital angular momentum \(\hat L\)Yes â€” \(\ell=0,1,2,\dots\) (s, p, d, â€¦)a genuine, varying, measured observable
Total spin of a composite (e.g. two electrons)Yes â€” singlet \(s{=}0\) or triplet \(s{=}1\)a genuine, varying, measured observable

For an electron, \(\hat S^2 = \tfrac34\hbar^2\) in every state, always. You never get different outcomes measuring it â€” it is a defining property of the particle species, effectively a superselection label. So the "magnitude of spin" is not a dynamical observable you measure; it is the name of the representation the particle's spin lives in. The components \(\hat S_z,\hat S_x,\dots\) are the operators that act within that representation â€” and those are exactly the things you do measure (a Sternâ€“Gerlach magnet reads \(\hat S_z\)). Components are dynamical; the magnitude is the label. That is your distinction, made exact.

The one idea

Observables are the self-adjoint generators of the system's symmetry group â€” momentum from translations, angular momentum from rotations, energy from time. The Casimir invariants (which commute with every generator) do not vary; they label the irreducible representation â€” i.e. what kind of thing the system is. So \(\hat J^2\) is an observable, but as a Casimir it is a fixed label: an elementary particle's spin-magnitude never changes, while its components â€” the generators â€” are the dynamical observables you actually measure.

A picture: the vector on a cone

The numbers even warn you off a definite vector. The length is \(|\mathbf J| = \sqrt{j(j+1)}\,\hbar\), which is strictly greater than the largest possible projection \(J_z^{\max} = j\hbar\) (since \(j(j+1)>j^2\)). The vector can therefore never fully lie along \(z\): with \(\hat J_z=m\hbar\) sharp, \(\hat J_x\) and \(\hat J_y\) have zero mean but non-zero spread â€” the vector is smeared around a cone about the \(z\)-axis. The magnitude is definite (Casimir), one projection is definite (\(J_z\)), and the direction is irreducibly fuzzy. There is no "actual arrow" hiding underneath.

The capstone: particles are the representations

The deepest instance of "magnitude as a fixed label" is Wigner's 1939 classification. An elementary particle simply is an irreducible unitary representation of the PoincarĂ© group (the symmetry group of relativistic spacetime), and irreducible representations are labeled by that group's two Casimir invariants:

\[ \hat P^2 = m^2 \quad(\text{mass}), \qquad \hat W^2 = -\,m^2\,j(j+1)\quad(\text{spin}). \]

So "the electron has mass \(m_e\) and spin \(\tfrac12\)" is literally a statement of which representation the electron is â€” two Casimir eigenvalues naming the irrep. Mass and spin, the two properties of a particle that never change, are precisely the Casimirs; momentum, position and spin-component, the things that vary and get measured, are the non-commuting generators acting within the representation. The same logic that says "spin components are observables but spin magnitude is a fixed label" is, at bottom, the organizing principle of particle physics.
