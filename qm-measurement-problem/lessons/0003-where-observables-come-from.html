Where Observables Come From: Symmetry, and the Angular-Momentum Puzzle

What principle decides which operators are physical observables? The answer is symmetry: observables are the generators of the system's symmetry transformations. Angular momentum is the perfect test case — and it resolves your puzzle about components versus magnitude.

The question — with one spoiler correction

You asked why there are observables for the components of angular momentum but (seemingly) not for their magnitude. First, a correction that turns out to be the whole story: the magnitude-squared \(\hat J^2\) is an observable — but a very special one (the Casimir), and for the spin of a given particle it is a fixed label, not a variable. Underneath sits the deeper question — what determines the observables at all? — and the single answer to both is: the symmetry group of the system.

The gap in the textbook answer

Intro QM says: an observable is a self-adjoint operator (real eigenvalues, orthogonal eigenbasis). True — but hopelessly non-selective. The self-adjoint operators on a Hilbert space form a vast sea; almost none of them correspond to anything you would call a physical quantity. Self-adjointness is a necessary condition, not the source. Something must single out position, momentum, energy, angular momentum, spin, charge as the observables of nature. That something is symmetry.

Observables are the generators of symmetries

A continuous symmetry acts on states by a family of unitary operators \(U(s)\). By Stone's theorem every such family is generated by a self-adjoint operator \(\hat G\):

\[ U(s) = e^{-\,i\,s\,\hat G/\hbar}. \]

That generator \(\hat G\) is an observable — self-adjoint by construction — and (Noether, quantum version) it is conserved exactly when the symmetry commutes with the dynamics, \([\hat G,\hat H]=0\). So the fundamental observables are not chosen by hand; they are read off from the symmetry group the physics respects:

Symmetry transformationIts generator = the observable
Spatial translationsmomentum \(\hat{\mathbf p}\)
Rotationsangular momentum \(\hat{\mathbf J}\)
Time translationenergy \(\hat H\) (the Hamiltonian)
Galilei boosts(mass-weighted) position \(m\hat{\mathbf x}\)
Internal / gauge phasecharge \(\hat Q\)

This is the modern, symmetry-first view of quantum mechanics (Wigner; developed cleanly by Ballentine): the whole list of "canonical" observables — \(\mathbf x,\mathbf p,\mathbf J,H\) — and even their commutation relations follow from projectively representing the spacetime symmetry group (Galilei non-relativistically, Poincaré relativistically) on Hilbert space. You don't postulate \([\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar\); you derive it from the group.

Angular momentum = the generators of rotation

Apply this to rotations. A rotation by angle \(\theta\) about axis \(\hat{\mathbf n}\) acts as \(U=e^{-\,i\,\theta\,\hat{\mathbf n}\cdot\hat{\mathbf J}/\hbar}\), so there are three generators \(\hat J_x,\hat J_y,\hat J_z\) — one per rotation axis. Their algebra is not an assumption; it is forced by the geometry of rotations, which fail to commute (rotating about \(x\) then \(y\) differs from \(y\) then \(x\)):

\[ [\hat J_i,\hat J_j] = i\hbar\,\epsilon_{ijk}\,\hat J_k . \]

Each \(\hat J_i\) is self-adjoint, hence each is a bona fide observable. That is why the components are observables: they are the generators of rotations. There is nothing arbitrary about them — remove rotational symmetry and they would not exist as a distinguished triple.

Why you can't have all three components at once

Because the generators do not commute, they share no common eigenbasis: no state can be an eigenstate of \(\hat J_x\), \(\hat J_y\) and \(\hat J_z\) together (except the trivial \(\mathbf J=0\)). The algebra directly forces an uncertainty relation,

\[ \Delta J_x\,\Delta J_y \;\ge\; \tfrac{\hbar}{2}\,\big|\langle \hat J_z\rangle\big| , \]

so at most one component can be sharp — conventionally \(\hat J_z\). The full vector \(\hat{\mathbf J}\), with a simultaneously definite \(x\), \(y\) and \(z\), is simply not a measurable object. (Notice this "uncertainty" is a fact about the symmetry algebra, not about a clumsy measuring device.)

The magnitude is an observable — the Casimir

Now the magnitude. Form \(\hat J^2 = \hat J_x^2+\hat J_y^2+\hat J_z^2\). A short computation with the algebra above gives

\[ [\hat J^2,\hat J_i] = 0 \quad\text{for every } i . \]

\(\hat J^2\) commutes with all the generators — it is the Casimir invariant of the rotation algebra. So, unlike the individual components, \(\hat J^2\) can be measured together with one component. The standard complete set of commuting observables is \(\{\hat J^2,\hat J_z\}\), with the familiar spectrum

\[ \hat J^2 \to \hbar^2\,j(j+1), \qquad \hat J_z \to \hbar\,m,\quad m=-j,\dots,+j . \]

So the magnitude-squared is very much an observable. But being the Casimir gives it a completely different role from the components: it commutes with everything, so it is constant on each irreducible representation. It does not point in any direction; it labels which representation you are in — the value of \(j\).

"But it's a function of non-commuting components — how is that allowed?"

The natural objection: \(\hat J^2\) is built from \(\hat J_x,\hat J_y,\hat J_z\), which don't commute — so how can it be a clean observable? The key is that non-commutativity doesn't stop you from forming operators; it stops you from making them jointly sharp. You may always add and multiply operators. And \(\hat J^2=\hat J_x^2+\hat J_y^2+\hat J_z^2\) is a sum of squares — each \(\hat J_i^2\) is just \(\hat J_i\) times itself, with no ordering ambiguity — so it is a perfectly well-defined, self-adjoint, positive operator. (Contrast a cross-term like \(\hat J_x\hat J_y\), which is order-ambiguous.)

Why does it then commute with each component? Watch the cancellation, using \([\hat J_x,\hat J_z]=-i\hbar\hat J_y\) and \([\hat J_y,\hat J_z]=+i\hbar\hat J_x\):

\[ [\hat J_x^2,\hat J_z] = -i\hbar(\hat J_x\hat J_y+\hat J_y\hat J_x),\qquad [\hat J_y^2,\hat J_z] = +i\hbar(\hat J_y\hat J_x+\hat J_x\hat J_y), \]

and \([\hat J_z^2,\hat J_z]=0\); the two cross-terms are equal and opposite, so \([\hat J^2,\hat J_z]=0\). The non-commutativity is exactly what makes them cancel. Conceptually: commuting with the generators \(\hat J_i\) is the same statement as being invariant under the rotations they generate — and a vector's length is rotation-invariant. So \(\hat J^2\) is special because it is the rotational invariant; a generic function of the components neither commutes with them nor is invariant.

Nor is there any contradiction with "you can't have all three components." Measuring the compatible set \(\{\hat J^2,\hat J_z\}\) makes

\[ \hat J_x^2+\hat J_y^2 = \hat J^2-\hat J_z^2 = \big[j(j+1)-m^2\big]\hbar^2 \]

perfectly sharp — yet \(\hat J_x\) and \(\hat J_y\) individually stay maximally uncertain. A particular combination of non-commuting observables can be definite even when the observables themselves cannot: definite length, definite \(\hat J_z\), fuzzy direction — precisely the cone below.

The resolution: for spin, the magnitude is a fixed label

Here is the precise sense in which your intuition was right. Whether \(\hat J^2\) behaves as a "variable you measure" or a "fixed property" depends on whether the physical system can occupy different irreducible representations:

CaseCan \(j\) change?So \(\hat J^2\) is…
Orbital angular momentum \(\hat L\)Yes — \(\ell=0,1,2,\dots\) (s, p, d, …)a genuine, varying, measured observable
Total spin of a composite (e.g. two electrons)Yes — singlet \(s{=}0\) or triplet \(s{=}1\)a genuine, varying, measured observable
Intrinsic spin of an elementary particleNo — an electron is always \(s=\tfrac12\)a fixed label, never a variable

For an electron, \(\hat S^2 = \tfrac34\hbar^2\) in every state, always. You never get different outcomes measuring it — it is a defining property of the particle species, effectively a superselection label. So the "magnitude of spin" is not a dynamical observable you measure; it is the name of the representation the particle's spin lives in. The components \(\hat S_z,\hat S_x,\dots\) are the operators that act within that representation — and those are exactly the things you do measure (a Stern–Gerlach magnet reads \(\hat S_z\)). Components are dynamical; the magnitude is the label. That is your distinction, made exact.

The one idea

Observables are the self-adjoint generators of the system's symmetry group — momentum from translations, angular momentum from rotations, energy from time. The Casimir invariants (which commute with every generator) do not vary; they label the irreducible representation — i.e. what kind of thing the system is. So \(\hat J^2\) is an observable, but as a Casimir it is a fixed label: an elementary particle's spin-magnitude never changes, while its components — the generators — are the dynamical observables you actually measure.

A picture: the vector on a cone

The numbers even warn you off a definite vector. The length is \(|\mathbf J| = \sqrt{j(j+1)}\,\hbar\), which is strictly greater than the largest possible projection \(J_z^{\max} = j\hbar\) (since \(j(j+1)>j^2\)). The vector can therefore never fully lie along \(z\): with \(\hat J_z=m\hbar\) sharp, \(\hat J_x\) and \(\hat J_y\) have zero mean but non-zero spread — the vector is smeared around a cone about the \(z\)-axis. The magnitude is definite (Casimir), one projection is definite (\(J_z\)), and the direction is irreducibly fuzzy. There is no "actual arrow" hiding underneath.

The capstone: particles are the representations

The deepest instance of "magnitude as a fixed label" is Wigner's 1939 classification. An elementary particle simply is an irreducible unitary representation of the Poincaré group (the symmetry group of relativistic spacetime), and irreducible representations are labeled by that group's two Casimir invariants:

\[ \hat P^2 = m^2 \quad(\text{mass}), \qquad \hat W^2 = -\,m^2\,j(j+1)\quad(\text{spin}). \]

So "the electron has mass \(m_e\) and spin \(\tfrac12\)" is literally a statement of which representation the electron is — two Casimir eigenvalues naming the irrep. Mass and spin, the two properties of a particle that never change, are precisely the Casimirs; momentum, position and spin-component, the things that vary and get measured, are the non-commuting generators acting within the representation. The same logic that says "spin components are observables but spin magnitude is a fixed label" is, at bottom, the organizing principle of particle physics.

Stepping back: what can't be an observable (but could classically)

Classically an observable is any real function on phase space: all such functions commute, all have simultaneous definite values, all are automatically real. Quantum mechanics imposes three filters a classical observable never has to pass — it must be a self-adjoint operator, mutually measurable observables must commute, and it must respect superselection. Several perfectly good classical observables don't survive:

Classical observableQuantum fateWhy
A phase-space point — \(x\) and \(p\) both sharpForbidden\([\hat x,\hat p]=i\hbar\neq0\): non-commuting, so never jointly sharp. The most basic classical observable is not a quantum one.
A generic function \(f(x,p)\)No unique / no consistent operatorOrdering ambiguity (\(\hat x\hat p\neq\hat p\hat x\)); and the Groenewold–van Hove theorem shows no quantization can promote all polynomials in \(x,p\) while preserving the bracket structure — only a small subalgebra (the symmetry generators and low-degree combinations) lifts cleanly.
Radial momentum \(p_r\)Not an observable\(\hat p_r\) on the half-line \(r>0\) is symmetric but has no self-adjoint extension — real-valued isn't enough; the operator must be genuinely self-adjoint.
Phase of an oscillatorNot an observableNo self-adjoint phase operator is conjugate to the number operator \(\hat N\) (whose spectrum is bounded below) — the Susskind–Glogower problem.
Time (e.g. time-of-arrival)Not an observablePauli's theorem: a self-adjoint \(\hat T\) with \([\hat T,\hat H]=i\hbar\) would force \(\hat H\) unbounded below. Time is a parameter, not an operator.
Relative phase across a superselection sector (charge, mass, boson/fermion)ForbiddenThe Hilbert space splits into sectors with no coherent superposition between them; an operator linking two sectors (e.g. reading the phase between a charge-0 and charge-1 state) is not physical.

The unifying reason is exactly the theme of this lesson read in reverse. A classical observable is just a real function on phase space, so it faces none of these constraints — everything commutes, everything is jointly defined, there are no sectors and no self-adjointness subtlety. A quantum observable must be a self-adjoint operator, and the physically admissible ones are cut down further by compatibility (commutation) and superselection. So the passage from classical to quantum doesn't merely blur values — it genuinely removes observables: the ones that can't be made self-adjoint, can't be jointly sharp, or can't respect the sector structure.

Primary sources

The cleanest development of "observables come from the symmetry group" is Ballentine, Quantum Mechanics: A Modern Development, ch. 3 (generators of the Galilei group; identification of operators with dynamical variables). For angular momentum as the generators of rotation and the \(\{\hat J^2,\hat J_z\}\) structure, Sakurai & Napolitano, Modern Quantum Mechanics, ch. 3. For the capstone — particles as Poincaré irreps labeled by mass and spin — Weinberg, The Quantum Theory of Fields, Vol. 1, ch. 2, and Wigner's original 1939 paper "On Unitary Representations of the Inhomogeneous Lorentz Group." On which classical observables fail to become quantum ones — self-adjoint versus merely symmetric operators, and the radial-momentum and phase-operator surprises — see Gieres, "Mathematical surprises and Dirac's formalism in quantum mechanics" (Rep. Prog. Phys. 63, 1893, 2000); the impossibility of consistently quantizing all of \(f(x,p)\) is the Groenewold–van Hove theorem.
