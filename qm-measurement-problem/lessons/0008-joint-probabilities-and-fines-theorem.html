When Does a Joint Probability Exist? Commutativity & Fine's Theorem

The Born rule gives \(P(a)\) for one observable; Lüders' rule gives sequential statistics. But is there a genuine joint distribution \(P(a,b)\) for two observables at once? The answer — exactly when they commute — runs from a two-line proof through quasi-probability negativity all the way to Bell's theorem, which Arthur Fine recast as the non-existence of such a distribution.

Why this lesson

Earlier lessons gave single-observable statistics (0003) and the measurement update (0007). This one is the joint-statistics story, and it ties three things usually taught separately into one thread: (i) commuting observables have a real joint distribution — non-commuting ones do not; (ii) the "distributions" you can still write down for incompatible observables go negative; (iii) in a Bell experiment, the existence of a joint distribution is equivalent to the Bell inequalities — Fine's theorem. The obstruction to a joint probability is quantum non-classicality.

The precise question

Fix a state \(\rho\) and two observables with spectral projectors \(A=\sum_a a\,P^A_a\), \(B=\sum_b b\,P^B_b\). We do not merely want some table of numbers \(P(a,b)\ge0\) summing to the two marginals — any pair of marginals has infinitely many such tables (the product, for one). We want a joint observable: a single measurement, described by a family of effects \(E_{ab}\ge 0\) with \(\sum_{ab}E_{ab}=\mathbb 1\), that returns the pair \((a,b)\) at once and whose two marginals are genuinely the measurements of \(A\) and \(B\), in every state:

\[ \sum_b E_{ab}=P^A_a,\qquad \sum_a E_{ab}=P^B_b,\qquad P_\rho(a,b)=\mathrm{Tr}(\rho\,E_{ab}). \]

Call the observables jointly measurable if such an \(E_{ab}\) exists. If the \(E_{ab}\) are moreover orthogonal projectors (a sharp joint observable), we ask the strongest version.

Commuting \(\iff\) a genuine (sharp) joint distribution

The exact criterion, with both directions provable in a few lines.

Theorem

A sharp joint observable \(\{E_{ab}\}\) with marginals \(P^A_a\) and \(P^B_b\) exists if and only if \([A,B]=0\) — and then it is forced to be \(E_{ab}=P^A_a P^B_b\).

(\(\Leftarrow\)) Commuting gives one. If \([A,B]=0\) then \([P^A_a,P^B_b]=0\), so each \(P^A_a P^B_b\) is again a projector, and \(\{P^A_a P^B_b\}\) is an orthogonal resolution of the identity: \(\sum_{ab}P^A_aP^B_b=\big(\sum_aP^A_a\big)\big(\sum_bP^B_b\big)=\mathbb 1\). Set \(E_{ab}=P^A_aP^B_b\); the marginal is \(\sum_b P^A_aP^B_b=P^A_a\big(\sum_bP^B_b\big)=P^A_a\) (and likewise for \(B\)). So \(P_\rho(a,b)=\mathrm{Tr}(\rho\,P^A_aP^B_b)\ge 0\) is an honest joint probability with the right marginals.

(\(\Rightarrow\)) A sharp joint observable forces commuting. Suppose orthogonal projectors \(E_{ab}\) (so \(E_{ab}E_{a'b'}=\delta_{aa'}\delta_{bb'}E_{ab}\)) have those marginals. Then

\[ P^A_a\,P^B_{b} = \Big(\sum_{b'}E_{ab'}\Big)\Big(\sum_{a'}E_{a'b}\Big) = \sum_{a',b'}E_{ab'}E_{a'b} = E_{ab}, \]

because orthogonality kills every term except \(a'=a,\ b'=b\). Running the two factors in the other order gives, identically, \(P^B_{b}\,P^A_a=E_{ab}\). Hence \(P^A_a P^B_b=P^B_b P^A_a\) for all \(a,b\), so \(A\) and \(B\) commute. \(\;\blacksquare\)

So "does \(P(a,b)\) exist?" has a crisp operational answer: a joint measurement of \(A\) and \(B\) is possible exactly when they are compatible. This is why the complete set of commuting observables of lesson 0003 is the right notion of "a maximal set of simultaneously definite quantities."

A caveat that matters. If you drop sharpness and allow unsharp \(E_{ab}\ge0\) that are not projectors (POVMs), some non-commuting pairs become jointly measurable — at the price of added noise, their marginals being smeared versions of \(A\) and \(B\) (Arthurs–Kelly joint position–momentum measurement is the classic case). Sharp joint measurability is equivalent to commuting; approximate joint measurability is a graded, noise-limited affair. The lesson's statements are about the sharp case unless said otherwise.

Non-commuting: the distribution goes negative

If you insist on a formal joint distribution for non-commuting \(A,B\) — a fixed rule, linear in \(\rho\), with the correct marginals in every state — you can write one, but it stops being a probability. The Kirkwood–Dirac distribution

\[ Q_\rho(a,b)=\mathrm{Tr}\big(\rho\,P^B_b P^A_a\big) \]

has exactly the right marginals — \(\sum_a Q_\rho(a,b)=\mathrm{Tr}(\rho\,P^B_b)=P_B(b)\) and \(\sum_b Q_\rho(a,b)=\mathrm{Tr}(\rho\,P^A_a)=P_A(a)\) — but its "effects" \(P^B_bP^A_a\) are not positive (not even Hermitian when \([A,B]\neq0\)), so \(Q_\rho(a,b)\) is in general complex or negative. The continuous-variable analogue is the Wigner function \(W(x,p)\): correct \(x\) and \(p\) marginals, but negative in patches for every non-classical state (by Hudson's theorem it is non-negative only for Gaussian pure states).

That negativity is not a defect of a clumsy definition — it is forced, and it is meaningful: Wigner (and Kirkwood–Dirac) negativity is a diagnostic of non-classicality and a genuine resource — necessary for quantum computational speed-up ("magic") and tied to contextuality. The impossibility of a bona fide \(P(a,b)\) for incompatible observables and the power of quantum mechanics are the same fact.

Fine's theorem: joint probability \(=\) Bell inequalities

Now the deepest turn, and the one you asked about. Move from one pair to a Bell/CHSH experiment: Alice measures \(A_1\) or \(A_2\) (outcomes \(\pm1\)), Bob measures \(B_1\) or \(B_2\) (outcomes \(\pm1\)); the four cross-pairs \((A_i,B_j)\) commute (spacelike separated) and are measured, giving correlations \(\langle A_iB_j\rangle\). The same-wing pairs \(A_1,A_2\) (and \(B_1,B_2\)) do not commute and are never measured together. Ask: is there one joint distribution \(P(a_1,a_2,b_1,b_2)\) over all four returning the four measured correlations?

Arthur Fine (PRL 48, 291, 1982; and J. Math. Phys. 23, 1306) proved that five statements are equivalent:

  1. a deterministic local hidden-variable model exists;
  2. a factorizable stochastic (local) model exists;
  3. a single joint distribution \(P(a_1,a_2,b_1,b_2)\) exists, returning the measured pairwise correlations;
  4. well-defined, mutually compatible joint distributions exist for all the pairs and triples (including the non-commuting same-wing ones);
  5. the Bell/CHSH inequalities hold.

The easy directions frame the result; the hard ones are Fine's. (3\(\Rightarrow\)5) is a one-line algebraic bound: if the four \(\pm1\)-valued quantities have a joint distribution, then pointwise

\[ S = a_1b_1 + a_1b_2 + a_2b_1 - a_2b_2 = a_1(b_1+b_2)+a_2(b_1-b_2) = \pm 2, \]

since for \(b_1,b_2\in\{\pm1\}\) one of \(b_1\pm b_2\) is \(0\) and the other is \(\pm2\). Averaging, \(|\langle S\rangle|=\big|\langle A_1B_1\rangle+\langle A_1B_2\rangle+\langle A_2B_1\rangle-\langle A_2B_2\rangle\big|\le 2\) — the CHSH inequality. (1\(\Rightarrow\)3) is also easy: a hidden variable \(\lambda\) assigning definite values \(a_i(\lambda),b_j(\lambda)\) induces a joint distribution by integrating over \(\lambda\). Fine's real work was the converses — (5\(\Rightarrow\)3), explicitly constructing a joint distribution from correlations that obey all the CHSH inequalities, and (2\(\Rightarrow\)1), reducing any stochastic local model to a deterministic one. Chaining them closes the loop.

The consequence is stark. Quantum mechanics reaches \(|\langle S\rangle| = 2\sqrt2\) (Tsirelson's bound), violating CHSH. By Fine's equivalence, that violation is identical to the statement that no joint distribution \(P(a_1,a_2,b_1,b_2)\) exists. Bell's theorem, read through Fine, is not first of all about "spooky action": it is that the incompatible observables of a Bell experiment cannot be embedded in a single classical probability space.

The marginal problem, and a caution

Structurally this is a marginal problem (Vorob'ev): you are handed the joint distributions of overlapping compatible contexts \(\{A_i,B_j\}\) and asked whether they glue into one global distribution. Bell/CHSH is exactly the gluing (consistency) condition, and quantum contextuality/nonlocality is its failure — the modern sheaf-theoretic view of Abramsky–Brandenburger. Caution — interpretation, not fact: Fine used this to argue that Bell's theorem is "really" about joint distributions rather than locality. That reading is influential but contested; most physicists hold that the locality assumption is doing essential work, and that dropping it (superdeterminism, retrocausality) is what the no-joint-distribution result actually costs you. State the theorem as a theorem; flag the moral as a debate.

How it is verified experimentally

  • Loophole-free Bell tests. The direct experimental confirmation that no joint distribution exists is the observation of a genuine CHSH violation with the detection and locality loopholes closed — achieved in 2015 by Hensen et al. (Delft, entangled NV-centre electron spins), and by Giustina et al. and Shalm et al. (Vienna and NIST, entangled photons). By Fine's theorem, each such violation is a demonstration that the four observables admit no common joint probability.
  • Directly measured negativity. The quasi-probabilities themselves have been reconstructed and shown to go negative: Wigner-function negativity is routinely mapped in cavity QED, trapped ions, and optical tomography, and Kirkwood–Dirac negativity/nonreality has been read out via weak measurements. You can literally watch the "joint distribution" for incompatible observables dip below zero.

The one idea

A joint probability for two quantum observables is not always available: a sharp one exists precisely when they commute (proved above, both ways), and when they don't, the best you can write is a quasi-probability that must go negative. Scale this up to a Bell experiment and the existence of a joint distribution over the incompatible observables becomes equivalent to the Bell inequalities and to local hidden variables — Fine's theorem. So quantum mechanics' refusal to supply joint probabilities for incompatible quantities is not a technical nuisance; it is the same thing as Wigner negativity, as contextuality, and as the violation of Bell's inequality — the signature that the quantum world has no single classical sample space underneath it.
