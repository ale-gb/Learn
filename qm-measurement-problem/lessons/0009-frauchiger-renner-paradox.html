Lesson 0009 · Foundations of QM

The Frauchiger–Renner Paradox: When Quantum Theory Is Turned on Its Own Users

Relational reasoning in quantum mechanics is consistent — as long as you only ever compare outcomes through actual physical interactions between commuting, jointly-measurable observables. Frauchiger and Renner (2018) found the breaking point: let agents apply quantum theory to each other, treat every measurement outcome as an absolute fact, and chain their certain inferences across frames that never physically meet — and quantum mechanics contradicts itself. This lesson builds the consistency result first, then shows exactly where and why it fails.

Why this lesson

This is the multi-agent sequel to two earlier threads. From lesson 0008: commuting observables have a genuine joint distribution — you will see Part 1 is that theorem, worn as "relational consistency." From lesson 0007: the projection postulate is what one observer applies to their own measurement while treating a distant observer unitarily. Frauchiger–Renner (FR) is what happens when those two moves — collapse for me, unitary for you — are pushed to a nested extreme. The payoff is a theorem sharper than Wigner's friend: no interpretation keeps all of "quantum theory is universal," "facts are absolute," and "agents' conclusions compose." At least one must go.

Part 1 — Relational consistency: why single-observer reasoning recovers the Born rule

Start with the reassuring half. Two parties, Alice and Bob, share \(\mathcal H_A\otimes\mathcal H_B\) in an arbitrary (possibly entangled) state \(|\Psi\rangle\). Alice measures a local observable with orthogonal projectors \(\{P_a\}\) on \(\mathcal H_A\); Bob measures \(\{Q_b\}\) on \(\mathcal H_B\). Because they act on different tensor factors, they commute exactly:

\[ [\,P_a\otimes I_B,\; I_A\otimes Q_b\,]=0 . \]

We now compute everything from Alice's reference frame: Alice collapses her own state (she made a measurement — Lüders), but she has not interacted with Bob, so relative to her, Bob's measurement is not a collapse — it is just a unitary that couples Bob's system to his memory register.

Step 1 — Alice measures. She obtains \(a\) with probability \(p(a)=\langle\Psi|(P_a\otimes I_B)|\Psi\rangle\), and updates her description of the whole universe by the projection postulate:

\[ |\Psi_a\rangle=\frac{(P_a\otimes I_B)|\Psi\rangle}{\sqrt{p(a)}} . \]

Step 2 — Bob measures, unitarily (from Alice's view). Bob's apparatus enacts \(U_B=I_A\otimes U_{S_B\to\text{mem}}\), entangling his spin with his notebook. Alice, having not looked, simply evolves her state: \(|\Psi_a'\rangle=U_B|\Psi_a\rangle\).

Step 3 — Alice walks into Bob's lab and reads his notebook for outcome \(b\). That reading is a projector \(\Pi_b\) on Bob's memory register. The probability she finds \(b\), given she already got \(a\), is \(p(b|a)=\langle\Psi_a'|(I_A\otimes\Pi_b)|\Psi_a'\rangle\).

Now substitute and simplify. Because \(P_a\otimes I_B\) acts only on \(A\) while \(U_B,\Pi_b\) act only on \(B\), everything on the two wings commutes, and \(P_a^\dagger P_a=P_a\):

\[ p(b|a)=\frac{1}{p(a)}\,\langle\Psi|\,P_a\otimes\big(U_B^\dagger\,\Pi_b\,U_B\big)\,|\Psi\rangle . \]

The Heisenberg-evolved notebook projector \(U_B^\dagger\Pi_b U_B\) is exactly Bob's original system observable \(Q_b\) — this is what "an ideal measurement" means: the pointer ends up perfectly correlated with \(Q_b\) (the same isometric premeasurement we lean on again below). Hence

\[ p(a,b)=p(a)\,p(b|a)=\langle\Psi|(P_a\otimes Q_b)|\Psi\rangle . \]

The consistency theorem — and its debt to lesson 0008

Computed honestly from one observer's frame, the relational joint statistics \(p(a,b)\) equal the ordinary global Born rule for the commuting pair \(P_a\otimes Q_b\). There is no frame-dependence, no paradox. And this is precisely the joint-measurability theorem of lesson 0008: because \([P_a\otimes I,\,I\otimes Q_b]=0\), a genuine sharp joint observable exists — it is \(E_{ab}=P_a\otimes Q_b\) — and \(p(a,b)=\mathrm{Tr}\!\big(|\Psi\rangle\langle\Psi|\,E_{ab}\big)\) is an honest probability. Microcausality (spacelike operators commute) is what guarantees that different agents' physically-compared observations glue into one consistent story.

Hold onto the italicised clause: physically compared, and commuting. Alice learned Bob's result by physically entering his lab and projecting onto his notebook — a real interaction between operators that commute. The entire consistency rests on that. Frauchiger–Renner is engineered to violate both conditions at once: it chains inferences across measurements that are never jointly performed, on the same wing, where the relevant operators do not commute.

Part 2 — Frauchiger–Renner: nesting the friends

Now put observers inside the boxes. Four agents: a friend \(F_A\) and a friend \(F_B\) sealed in isolated labs, plus two "super-observers" Alice and Bob who can measure those entire labs coherently from outside. Two systems: a biased quantum coin \(R\) and a spin \(S\).

  • Prepare & branch. \(F_A\) measures the coin \(R=\sqrt{\tfrac13}\,|h\rangle+\sqrt{\tfrac23}\,|t\rangle\) in \(\{|h\rangle,|t\rangle\}\), then prepares the spin conditionally: \(h\Rightarrow|{\downarrow}\rangle\), and \(t\Rightarrow|{\rightarrow}\rangle=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(|{\uparrow}\rangle+|{\downarrow}\rangle)\), and sends \(S\) to \(F_B\).
  • The global state (writing \(\sqrt{\tfrac23}\cdot\tfrac1{\sqrt2}=\sqrt{\tfrac13}\)) is the little Hardy-type state \[ |\Psi\rangle=\sqrt{\tfrac13}\,\Big(\,|h\rangle|{\downarrow}\rangle+|t\rangle|{\uparrow}\rangle+|t\rangle|{\downarrow}\rangle\,\Big). \]
  • \(F_B\) measures \(S\) in \(\{|{\uparrow}\rangle,|{\downarrow}\rangle\}\) and records the result.
  • Alice measures \(F_A\)'s whole lab (coin + friend's memory) in the reversal basis \(|\text{ok}\rangle_A=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(|h\rangle-|t\rangle)\), \(|\text{fail}\rangle_A=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(|h\rangle+|t\rangle)\).
  • Bob measures \(F_B\)'s whole lab in \(|\text{ok}\rangle_B=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(|{\downarrow}\rangle-|{\uparrow}\rangle)\), \(|\text{fail}\rangle_B=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(|{\downarrow}\rangle+|{\uparrow}\rangle)\).
Those \(|\text{ok}\rangle\) measurements are the crux. Measuring \(F_A\)'s lab in a basis superposing \(|h\rangle\) and \(|t\rangle\) is a measurement that does not commute with \(F_A\)'s own \(\{|h\rangle,|t\rangle\}\) measurement — it partially undoes it. This is the same non-commutativity that Part 1 forbade. Physically it is a Wigner's-friend "reversal": interfering the branches of a sealed observer. That is why the FR labs must be perfectly isolated and coherent — and why the experiment is a thought experiment for macroscopic friends.

The chain of certainties

Assume, with FR, that each sealed measurement produces a single absolute fact that any agent may reason about. Then each agent draws a probability-one inference — these are certainties, not statistics, which is what makes the eventual clash lethal.

Conclusion 1 (Alice → spin). Rewrite \(|\Psi\rangle\) in Alice's basis using \(|h\rangle=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(|\text{fail}\rangle_A+|\text{ok}\rangle_A)\), \(|t\rangle=\tfrac1{\sqrt2}(|\text{fail}\rangle_A-|\text{ok}\rangle_A)\). Collecting terms,

\[ |\Psi\rangle=\underbrace{\sqrt{\tfrac16}\,|\text{fail}\rangle_A\big(|{\uparrow}\rangle+2|{\downarrow}\rangle\big)}_{\text{norm}^2=5/6}\;-\;\underbrace{\sqrt{\tfrac16}\,|\text{ok}\rangle_A\,|{\uparrow}\rangle}_{\text{norm}^2=1/6}. \]

In the \(|\text{ok}\rangle_A\) branch the two \(|{\downarrow}\rangle\) contributions (from \(|h\rangle|{\downarrow}\rangle\) and \(|t\rangle|{\downarrow}\rangle\)) cancel, leaving only \(|{\uparrow}\rangle\). So if Alice gets \(\text{ok}\), the spin is certainly \(|{\uparrow}\rangle\): \(A=\text{ok}\Rightarrow S={\uparrow}\).

Correcting a tempting shortcut. It is tempting to "simplify" the fail branch to \(\sqrt{\tfrac23}\,|\text{fail}\rangle_A|{\rightarrow}\rangle\). It is not \(|{\rightarrow}\rangle\): the internal spin state there is \((|{\uparrow}\rangle+2|{\downarrow}\rangle)/\sqrt5\), and \(\sqrt{\tfrac23}\,|{\rightarrow}\rangle-\tfrac1{\sqrt6}|\text{ok}\rangle|{\uparrow}\rangle\) is not even normalised (\(\tfrac23+\tfrac16=\tfrac56\neq1\)). Only the ok branch matters for the argument, and it is exactly \(-\tfrac1{\sqrt6}|\text{ok}\rangle_A|{\uparrow}\rangle\). Every conclusion below uses only that branch. (Verified symbolically and numerically before publishing.)

Conclusion 2 (\(F_B\) → coin). Look at the original \(|\Psi\rangle\): the only term containing \(|{\uparrow}\rangle\) is \(\sqrt{\tfrac13}\,|t\rangle|{\uparrow}\rangle\). So if the spin is \(|{\uparrow}\rangle\), the coin was certainly \(t\): \(S={\uparrow}\Rightarrow R=t\). (Equivalently: \(F_B\) reasons "I saw \({\uparrow}\), so \(R\) must have been \(t\).")

Conclusion 3 (\(F_A\) → Bob fails). If \(R=t\), then \(F_A\) knows she prepared \(S=|{\rightarrow}\rangle\). But Bob's \(\text{ok}\) outcome is orthogonal to that:

\[ \langle\text{ok}_B|{\rightarrow}\rangle=\tfrac12\big(\langle{\downarrow}|-\langle{\uparrow}|\big)\big(|{\uparrow}\rangle+|{\downarrow}\rangle\big)=\tfrac12(1-1)=0 . \]

So Bob cannot get \(\text{ok}\): \(R=t\Rightarrow B=\text{fail}\).

A rigor note the argument actually needs

The inner product \(\langle\text{ok}_B|{\rightarrow}\rangle\) was computed as if Bob measures the bare spin \(S\). By the time Bob acts, \(S\) has already been coupled to \(F_B\)'s memory. The shortcut is legitimate only if that premeasurement is an isometry — it embeds \(\mathcal H_S\) into the lab and preserves inner products, \(|{\uparrow}\rangle_S|0\rangle_{F_B}\mapsto|{\uparrow}\rangle_{F_B}\), \(|{\downarrow}\rangle_S|0\rangle_{F_B}\mapsto|{\downarrow}\rangle_{F_B}\) with the images orthonormal. Then \(\langle\text{ok}_B|{\rightarrow}\rangle=0\) transfers exactly to Bob's measurement on the whole lab. FR assume exactly this ideal (reversible, information-preserving) friend. It is the same ideal-measurement assumption that made \(U_B^\dagger\Pi_bU_B=Q_b\) work in Part 1 — worth stating out loud rather than silently measuring \(S\) as a stand-in for the lab.

Transitivity. Chain them: \(A=\text{ok}\;\Rightarrow\;S={\uparrow}\;\Rightarrow\;R=t\;\Rightarrow\;B=\text{fail}\). Every step is a certainty, so the composite is a certainty:

\[ A=\text{ok}\ \Longrightarrow\ B=\text{fail}\qquad\text{(they can never both get ok).} \]

The Born-rule contradiction

Now just ask quantum mechanics directly for the joint probability of both super-observers getting \(\text{ok}\). From Conclusion 1's rewrite, \(\langle\text{ok}_A|\Psi\rangle=-\tfrac1{\sqrt6}\,|{\uparrow}\rangle\). Project onto \(|\text{ok}\rangle_B\):

\[ \langle\text{ok}_A|\langle\text{ok}_B|\Psi\rangle=\Big(-\tfrac1{\sqrt6}\Big)\langle\text{ok}_B|{\uparrow}\rangle=\Big(-\tfrac1{\sqrt6}\Big)\Big(-\tfrac1{\sqrt2}\Big)=\tfrac1{\sqrt{12}}, \] \[ p(\text{ok}_A,\text{ok}_B)=\Big|\tfrac1{\sqrt{12}}\Big|^2=\tfrac1{12}\neq 0 . \]

The clash

The agents' chain of certain inferences says \(A=\text{ok}\Rightarrow B=\text{fail}\), i.e. \(p(\text{ok},\text{ok})=0\). The Born rule, applied to the very same experiment, says \(p(\text{ok},\text{ok})=\tfrac1{12}\). Run it \(12\) times and roughly once both super-observers announce "ok" — the outcome the logical chain declared impossible. Quantum theory, used to model agents who themselves use quantum theory, has contradicted itself.

What has to give: the three assumptions

FR distil the contradiction to three premises that cannot all hold. Naming them makes visible which one each inference secretly used.

  • (Q) Universality. Quantum theory applies to everything, including macroscopic agents and their labs — so Alice and Bob may model \(F_A,F_B\) unitarily and perform the \(|\text{ok}\rangle\) reversal measurements at all. (Used the moment we treat a sealed friend as a coherent quantum system.)
  • (C) Consistency. Different agents' conclusions can be Boolean-combined: if \(F_B\) is certain \(R=t\), Alice may import that certainty into her own chain. (Used at every "\(\Rightarrow\)" that crosses from one agent's frame to another's.)
  • (S) Single outcomes. Each measurement yields one absolute, observer-independent fact — so "the spin is \({\uparrow}\)" and "\(F_B\) saw \({\uparrow}\)" name the same settled event. (Used to treat each conclusion as a fact reasoners share.)

The theorem is that \(Q\wedge C\wedge S\) is inconsistent. So every interpretation of quantum mechanics must reject at least one:

  • Many-Worlds denies (S) — both outcomes occur; "the" fact of Alice's result isn't singular, so the chain never had shared facts to combine.
  • Bohmian mechanics restricts (Q) — the fundamental beables are particle positions, not the wavefunction of an observer; modelling a friend as a mere unitary object is where it balks.
  • Relational QM and QBism reject the absolute reading of (S) and the Boolean combination in (C) — facts are indexed to observers, and stitching one frame's certainties into another's is illegitimate until the frames physically intersect (precisely the licence Part 1 required and FR withholds).

Interpretation, not settled fact

The "which axiom does each interpretation drop" map above is a standard pedagogical heuristic, not a community consensus. Proponents dispute which of \(Q,C,S\) their framework really rejects; some argue a given view violates more than one at once, or that the \(Q/C/S\) slicing is not the most natural way to describe its commitments at all. What is rigorous is the theorem — \(Q\wedge C\wedge S\Rightarrow\bot\) — and the number \(p(\text{ok},\text{ok})=\tfrac1{12}\). Treat the taxonomy as a map for orientation, and expect experts to redraw its borders.

How Part 1 and Part 2 fit together

The two halves are one point seen twice. Part 1: when observers compare notes through real interactions between commuting observables, relational reasoning is provably consistent and reproduces the Born rule — this is lesson 0008's joint-distribution theorem in disguise. Part 2: FR builds a scenario whose lethal step is a measurement (the \(|\text{ok}\rangle\) reversal) that does not commute with a friend's earlier measurement and that is never jointly performed with the inferences chained to it. The moment you assume those cross-frame certainties compose anyway — as if a single joint distribution over all four incompatible outcomes existed — you are asserting exactly the classical embedding that Fine's theorem already told us quantum mechanics forbids. FR is Fine's "no global sample space," dramatised with observers standing in for the observables.

Lineage and sharper cousins

FR is the modern apex of the Wigner's-friend lineage (Wigner, 1961): an observer inside a box, and a super-observer who can interfere the box's branches. Two strengthenings are worth knowing. Brukner recast the tension as a no-go theorem for observer-independent facts using a Bell-type inequality, moving from FR's single deterministic run to a statistical, testable form. Bong et al. proved a stronger "local friendliness" no-go — pitting the absoluteness of observed events against local agency — and ran a proof-of-principle photonic test (with a single photon standing in for the "friend"). The through-line: the more carefully you insist that a sealed observer's outcome is an absolute fact and that quantum theory applies to that observer, the more sharply quantum correlations refuse to let those facts live in one shared classical world.

The one idea

Quantum theory reasons about agents flawlessly as long as their conclusions are compared through actual physical interactions between compatible, jointly-measurable observables — Part 1, which is the commuting-observables joint distribution of lesson 0008. Frauchiger–Renner is the demonstration that if you additionally demand that every sealed measurement is an absolute fact and that isolated agents' certain inferences may be chained across frames that never physically meet, you can derive \(p(\text{ok},\text{ok})=0\) while the Born rule delivers \(\tfrac1{12}\). Something in \(\{\)universality, single outcomes, composable facts\(\}\) must be surrendered. Quantum mechanics does not merely resist a God's-eye view of nature; turned on its own users, it forbids one.
